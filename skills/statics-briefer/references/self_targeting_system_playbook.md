# Self-Targeting System: Course Playbook

A systematic approach to avoiding ad creative fatigue by targeting the three psychological selves of your customer.

---

## Module 1: The Problem and Solution Overview

### The Core Problem: Angle Rut

Angle rut occurs when your ads repeat the same message, emotional appeal, and customer framing over and over. Symptoms include:

- Ads that look, feel, and drive the same results every time
- A small number of winning ads propping up the entire ad account
- Rising ad costs with diminishing returns
- Difficulty finding new winning angles
- Anxiety about what happens when top performers fatigue

This happens to brands at every scale, from startups to nine-figure companies. Once top performers stop working, recovery is extremely difficult—like slamming the brakes after you've already hit ice.

### The Solution: Target Three Different Selves

Your customer is not one person—they are three. When an ad appears, only one version of your customer's self is in control at that moment. Only one buys.

The key insight: You don't need a new offer, new landing page, or new product. You need to shift which self your ad speaks to. This lets you reach buyers who have been ignoring you because your current ads don't resonate with their active self.

### Why This Works

Great marketers don't panic when performance blends or "beiges out." They rotate creative before fatigue sets in. This system teaches you to do that scientifically, staying ahead of the curve before ads crash and burn.

## Module 2: Analysis Setup

### Before You Begin

You do not need hundreds of ads to run this analysis. Start with a small, manageable sample size. Running your entire ad account through analysis will likely overwhelm you and produce less actionable insights.

### The Nine-Ad Identity Scan

Select exactly nine ads for your initial analysis:

1. **Three Top Performers** - Ads that are clearly crushing it
2. **Three Mid Performers** - Ads that are okay but not scaling well (ones you're leaving on to see what happens)
3. **Three Clear Flops** - Ads that performed poorly

This distribution gives you a clean diagnostic set that reveals:
- Which self you're speaking to most often
- Which self is being ignored in your ad account
- Where opportunity exists to unlock net new audiences

### What This Analysis Reveals

As you run through this system, you'll build a clear picture of which angles you are overusing—not which ones customers respond to best, but which ones your mind defaults to when creating ads. Identifying your blind spots is how you get ahead of fatigue and expand your market from the inside out.

## Module 3: How the Brain Processes Ads

### Why This Psychology Matters

Understanding how the brain makes purchasing decisions explains why targeting different selves works and why angle rut happens in the first place.

### The 95% Subconscious Rule

According to neuroscience, 95% of decisions are made by the subconscious mind. This is a very old, reactive, emotional part of the brain. It's not analyzing information logically—it's making split-second decisions: "Should I engage with this or not?" If the answer is no, it dips out immediately.

### How the Brain Creates Shortcuts

The subconscious is constantly recording everything you experience—every interaction, every sensory input. It stores and processes this information so that next time it encounters a similar experience, it knows what to do automatically.

**Critical implication for advertising:** If your ads look the same and the brain has ignored them before, it will keep ignoring them. You're teaching it to ignore you.

### Why the Brain Is So Selective

The brain consumes roughly 20% of your daily energy (glucose). To conserve resources, it's extremely careful about what it spends attention on. Every day, the brain makes judgment calls about what deserves energy and what to ignore. Useful information gets stored in memory banks; everything else gets filtered out.

### How Memory Banks Become Identity

These memory banks are built on:
- **Experiences** → which create **Emotions** → which form **Memories** → which create **Identity Banks**

When you use these banks repeatedly without updating them, they become part of your programming—your habits, behaviors, and identities. These identity banks vary by person and activate differently depending on the products, brands, or offers encountered.

### Your Customer Is a System, Not a Static Avatar

Your customer type is actually hundreds of different avatars wrapped into one person. Everything in their life related to your product category is interconnected in their brain.

Example: For coffee, their brain connects what they drink at Starbucks, what beans they have at home, what their mother used to drink, what coffee she swore she'd never drink because her father drank it—all mapped together.

**Practical implication:** Some days your customer tries to become someone new. Other days they hold tight to who they are. Sometimes they're under pressure to meet expectations. Each state activates a different self, and each self responds to different messaging.

## Module 4: The Three Selves Explained

### The Psychology Foundation: Self-Discrepancy Theory

This system is built on Self-Discrepancy Theory, developed by psychologist E. Tory Higgins in 1987. The theory states: When there's a gap between versions of yourself, you feel discomfort. This discomfort motivates you to change—or buy things that make you feel closer to the version of yourself you want to be.

### The Three Selves

All three selves exist in your customer's subconscious at all times, but at different intensity levels. The self that's currently most active determines how they respond to your ad.

---

#### 1. IDEAL SELF

**Definition:** Who you want to be. Your dreams, hopes, and aspirations.

**Mindset:** Future-focused. Seeking potential, progress, and transformation.

**Responds best to messaging that:**
- Paints a vivid picture of a better future
- Promises a more fulfilled version of themselves
- Emphasizes growth and possibility

**Example phrases:**
- "Step into the version that's been waiting for you"
- "Unlock your next level"
- "Feel like yourself again"
- "What if this is the thing that finally works?"
- "Here's what your life could look like if you just said yes"

**What you're selling:** Hope, aspiration, the thrill of progress

**Caution:** Most brands default to Ideal Self messaging. It's aspirational and motivational, but this segment can tap out and dry up. There's not an infinite pool of people shopping in Ideal Self mode at any given time.

---

#### 2. ACTUAL SELF

**Definition:** Who you currently believe you are. Your existing identity.

**Mindset:** Present-focused. Seeking validation and consistency with current identity.

**Responds best to messaging that:**
- Reinforces who they already are
- Validates existing behaviors and choices
- Fits naturally into their current self-narrative

**Example phrases:**
- "You already know this works"
- "Made for people like you"
- "Stick to what feels right"
- "You're already this type of person—this product just fits"

**What you're selling:** Validation, reassurance, belonging

**Key difference from Ideal Self:** You're not trying to change or transform anyone. You're reinforcing their existing identity and positioning the product as a natural extension of who they already are.

---

#### 3. OUGHT SELF

**Definition:** Who you feel you should be. Based on duty, responsibility, and external expectations.

**Mindset:** Obligation-focused. Driven by standards, expectations, and doing the right thing.

**Responds best to messaging that:**
- Emphasizes safety and doing things correctly
- Invokes responsibility and standards
- Frames purchase as meeting obligations

**Example phrases:**
- "Don't let this fall through the cracks"
- "People are counting on you"
- "Do what your future self will thank you for"
- "Good for [identity]" (happy moms, good golfers, etc.)
- "You have a standard to uphold—this is how you meet it"

**What you're selling:** Relief, reassurance, meeting duty

**Caution:** Handle Ought Self carefully. It's about duty and standards, not dreams. The pressure can feel different—it's "should" energy rather than "want" energy.

---

### Quick Reference: Which Self Am I Targeting?

| Self | Time Focus | Core Drive | Emotion Sold | Key Word |
|------|------------|------------|--------------|----------|
| Ideal | Future | Aspiration | Hope/Progress | "Could" |
| Actual | Present | Validation | Belonging | "Are" |
| Ought | Obligation | Duty | Relief | "Should" |

### Why This Matters for Your Ads

Each self requires:
- Different emotional lens
- Different language patterns
- Different ad styles

Your job is not to reinvent your product. It's to reposition your message to meet customers where they already are psychologically. You've probably nailed one self already—now you need to unlock the other two.

## Module 5: Running the Diagnostic

### Purpose of the Three Selves Diagnostic

This diagnostic identifies:
- What versions of your customer you're already speaking to
- Which selves are missing from your ad account
- Opportunities to activate new buyer segments

### Tools Required

- ChatGPT (or another capable LLM)
- Your selected nine ads (screenshots for statics, transcripts for videos)
- Optionally: metric data from your ad platform

### If Running Video Ads

Transcribe your videos and run the script through analysis. You can also run screenshots of key frames, but starting with core messaging (the script) typically produces clearer results.

### Ad Examples by Self Type

**Actual Self Ad Example:**
> "He put up the tent. You brought the flask."

This reinforces existing identity—you're already the gear person, the one who brings the fun and ambiance. Language is calm, proven, tested.

**Ideal Self Ad Example:**
> "For Campfire Legends Only"

This says you could become this person if you had this product. Language is transformational, next-level, aspirational.

**Ought Self Ad Example:**
> "Details matter. Even outdoors."

This invokes responsibility and standards—you should be the type of person who cares about details everywhere. Language emphasizes protection, quality, and not letting standards slip.

### The Analysis Prompts

Two prompts are provided with this course:

**Prompt 1: Self-Discrepancy Analysis (No Metrics)**
- Analyzes ads and scores them 0-10 for each self type
- Provides summary of which self is strongest in each ad
- Explains reasoning for each score

**Prompt 2: Self-Discrepancy + Performance Analysis (With Metrics)**
- Same analysis as Prompt 1
- Cross-references with your performance metrics
- Identifies which self converts best
- Provides recommended ratios for future creative (e.g., "Shift to 50% Actual, 10% Ought, 40% Ideal")

### How the AI Analysis Works

The LLM analyzes:
- Headlines, subheads, badges, buttons, callouts
- Images (for static ads)
- Emotion, descriptive words, adjectives, verbs
- Intensity of emotional language

It assigns numerical scores based on how strongly each self is present in the ad's language and imagery.

**Important:** Give the AI clear boundaries. Request specific outputs: scores, numbers, percentages. Without these constraints, results may be inconsistent or vague.

### What the Output Looks Like

For each ad, you receive:
- Ad identifier (name or number)
- Actual Self score (0-10)
- Ideal Self score (0-10)  
- Ought Self score (0-10)
- Reasoning/notes for each score

Plus a summary that identifies:
- Which self you're activating most consistently
- Patterns across high vs. low performers
- Which self is underutilized (your biggest opportunity)

## Module 7: Rewriting Ads for Different Selves

### The Reframing Strategy

Now that you know which self your ads speak to, it's time to rewrite. You won't start from scratch—you'll take existing winners and reposition them for a different version of your customer.

This approach:
- Prevents angle rut
- Provides data on which self is most likely to buy
- Requires zero new creative assets (same imagery, different messaging)

### Critical Rule: Keep Everything Else Constant

When testing self-targeting, change only the messaging. Keep constant:
- The offer
- The landing page
- The CTA
- The price
- The discount/bundle structure

This is CRO-level discipline. Test one variable (the self) at a time to get clean data.

### Using the Rewrite Prompt

**Step 1:** Copy your winning ad into ChatGPT with the rewrite prompt

**Step 2:** The AI identifies which self the current ad targets

**Step 3:** The AI generates versions for the other two selves

**Output options:**
- Headline + subhead + social proof + bullet points (default)
- Full ad script (modify prompt to request this)

### Example: Three Versions of One Ad

**Original (Ideal Self):**
> "Spill the tea, not your wine"
> *(Future-focused, aspirational, "become this classy person")*

**Rewritten for Actual Self:**
> Messaging that validates wine lovers' existing identity and lifestyle

**Rewritten for Ought Self:**
> Messaging about responsibility, maintaining standards, not compromising on quality

### When to Preserve Core Messaging

If you have a core message that has worked consistently for years across multiple ads, that's a brand-level asset. Don't change it arbitrarily—document it and build on it strategically.

The self-targeting system is for situations where:
- Same message runs throughout the ad account
- Performance is inconsistent
- You can't identify why some iterations work and others don't

### What You're Actually Doing

You're not switching offers. You're switching who you're inviting in. You're opening an emotional door to a different version of your customer.

This unlocks new buyer types with zero extra creative cost. The algorithm does heavy lifting, but you'll get results faster and cheaper by activating different selves rather than just producing more volume.

## Module 8: Testing and Performance Patterns

### Testing Setup

**Step 1:** Take your three ad variants (one Actual, one Ideal, one Ought)

**Step 2:** Place all three in one ad set within one campaign

**Step 3:** Ensure the campaign will give these ads adequate spend

**Step 4:** Keep everything else identical:
- Same product
- Same landing page  
- Same price
- Same everything except the ad creative

**Step 5:** Run and watch for pattern differences

### What You're Testing For

You are NOT testing which ads convert best. Conversions are part of the puzzle but not everything.

You ARE testing for identity response:
- Who responds fastest?
- Who responds strongest?
- Who responds deepest?

### Performance Patterns by Self Type

Based on statistical analysis across many tests, here's what to expect:

---

#### ACTUAL SELF Performance Signals

- **CTR:** Lower than average
- **AOV:** Higher than average
- **Returns:** Very stable

**Why:** People in Actual Self mode aren't click-happy. They scroll with purpose, looking for specific content, then exit. But because their identity aligns strongly with the product, once they do engage, they commit at higher value.

---

#### IDEAL SELF Performance Signals

- **CTR:** High
- **AOV:** Lower than average
- **Engagement:** Lots of interaction, less commitment

**Why:** People in Ideal Self mode are exploratory. They're seeking answers, evaluating options, trying to figure things out. They click readily but haven't built trust with the product yet. Expect high engagement volume with lower per-customer value.

---

#### OUGHT SELF Performance Signals

- **Initial performance:** Slow start, hard to get traction
- **Intent:** Very strong once activated
- **Add-to-carts:** High
- **LTV:** High
- **Loyalty:** Very high

**Why:** Ought Self shoppers are careful and deliberate. They don't impulse-click. But once they attach to something out of duty or responsibility, they stay attached. These become your most loyal customers because they feel obligated to maintain the behavior.

**Patience required:** An Ought Self ad might run for two weeks with minimal traction, then suddenly hit. Don't kill it too early.

---

### Why Brands Stall Out

Most brands stall not because of laziness or lack of skill. They stall because they hit a wall—targeting the same version of the same customer repeatedly without realizing it.

Marketer bias is real. We all over-index on one type of messaging because it worked before, and we can't pull out of that pattern.

**Symptoms:**
- Ads working but not scaling
- Angles converting but fatiguing quickly
- Only one angle producing results
- Fear about what happens when that angle stops working

Narrowing in on a single identity is an unsafe way to scale.

### The Complete System

1. **Diagnose** who you're currently speaking to
2. **Reframe** angles for new emotional entry points
3. **Test** using systematized messaging that matches how customers see themselves in three different versions

This makes your ads more self-targeting and reduces angle fatigue.

## Troubleshooting

**Q: My analysis shows I'm 90% Ideal Self. Is that bad?**
A: It's not bad—it's common. Most marketers default to aspirational messaging. The insight is knowing you have two untapped audiences. Start testing Actual and Ought Self variants.

**Q: My Ought Self ad isn't getting any traction after a week.**
A: This is expected. Ought Self typically shows slow starts followed by strong, sustained performance. Give it at least 2-3 weeks before judging. Watch for add-to-cart signals even if conversions are low.

**Q: Should I stop running Ideal Self ads entirely?**
A: No. The goal is balance and rotation, not replacement. Continue running what works while adding new self-targeted variants to prevent fatigue.

**Q: How often should I re-run the diagnostic?**
A: Quarterly is a good baseline. Also re-run after major creative pushes or when you notice performance patterns shifting.

**Q: Can one ad target multiple selves?**
A: Ads often have a dominant self with traces of others. That's fine. For testing purposes, make each variant lean heavily into one self so you get clean data on what resonates.
