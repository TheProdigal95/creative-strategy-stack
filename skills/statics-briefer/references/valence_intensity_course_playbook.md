# Valence Intensity Model: Course Playbook

A framework for diagnosing why ads underperform and how to fix them by aligning emotional tone with customer psychology.

---

## Module 1: The Core Problem

### Why "Good" Ads Fail

When an ad has a strong offer, compelling headline, and polished creative but still doesn't convert, the issue is typically not a creative problem—it's an **emotional tone mismatch**.

**The real issue:** Your ad is misaligned with what your audience believes about their problem. The words may be right, but the *feeling* is wrong.

### Common Misdiagnosis

When ads underperform, most brands try:
- Changing targeting
- Converting to GIF format
- Hiring new UGC creators
- Drafting new hooks
- Endless tweaking

These rarely work because they address the wrong problem.

### The Actual Fix

Customers respond to emotional alignment, not just good copy. When your ad's emotional temperature doesn't match the moment your customer is in—or how they feel about their problem—even great offers fall flat.

**Key insight:** Most marketers optimize words. Winners optimize for feel.

---

## Module 2: Understanding Valence and Intensity

Every emotion in an ad exists on two dimensions. Mastering these dimensions is how you diagnose and fix emotional misfires.

### Valence: Which Emotion

Valence answers: **What emotion am I trying to get the customer to feel?**

**Positive valence** = Emotions that feel good
- Joy, pride, excitement, hope, comfort
- Function: Pulls people *toward* something they want
- Use case: Aspirational marketing

**Negative valence** = Emotions that feel bad
- Fear, frustration, anxiety, FOMO, anger
- Function: Pushes people *away* from things they fear or dislike
- Use case: Risk-based marketing (security, protection messaging)

### Intensity: How Hard You Hit

Intensity answers: **How deeply am I communicating this emotion?**

**High intensity** = Stronger emotional arousal
- Creates urgency and momentum
- Demands immediate attention
- Example: Rage vs. mild irritation

**Low intensity** = Softer emotional impact
- Builds trust, recognition, reflection
- Allows processing time
- Example: Gentle concern vs. panic

### The Relationship

- Valence = WHAT you're saying emotionally
- Intensity = HOW you're delivering it

Same emotion at different intensities produces completely different results.

## Module 3: The Four Emotional Zones

Map any ad onto a four-quadrant axis:
- Vertical axis: Valence (positive at top, negative at bottom)
- Horizontal axis: Intensity (low on left, high on right)

### Zone 1: High Valence, Low Intensity — "The Feel-Good Zone"

**Emotional character:** Reassuring, calm, supportive, warm

**Best for:**
- Wellness brands
- Luxury products
- Intimacy products
- Safety-focused products
- Belonging/community brands
- Any product where the customer wants to feel "okay inside"

**Typical emotions activated:** Comfort, gentle satisfaction, trust, security

---

### Zone 2: High Valence, High Intensity — "The Hype Zone"

**Emotional character:** Excitement, energy, thrill, celebration

**Best for:**
- Product launches
- Performance products/industries
- Achievement-focused messaging
- Momentum-building campaigns
- Any situation where you need customers to take physical action

**Typical emotions activated:** Joy, thrill, excitement, pride, anticipation

---

### Zone 3: Negative Valence, Low Intensity — "The Subtle Frustration Zone"

**Emotional character:** Quiet dissatisfaction, unmet expectations, low-grade discontent

**Best for:**
- Pain point building
- Before/after contrast setups
- Relief-focused messaging (setting up the "after")
- Problem awareness campaigns

**Typical emotions activated:** Annoyance, fatigue, irritation, disappointment, mild frustration

**Caution:** Many marketers default to this zone. Avoid it in saturated industries where competitors are already hitting the same pain points the same way.

---

### Zone 4: Negative Valence, High Intensity — "The Fear & Urgency Zone"

**Emotional character:** Alarm, scarcity, threat, time pressure

**Best for:**
- Limited-time offers
- Health and safety industries
- High-stakes decisions
- Loss-prevention messaging

**Typical emotions activated:** Panic, fear, anxiety, sense of loss, threat awareness

**Critical warning:** This zone requires careful handling. The goal is to connect with emotions around real problems—NOT to trigger trauma or make people feel bad artificially. Use this zone to help customers recognize that their existing emotional pain could be solved by your product. Never manufacture fear that isn't grounded in their actual experience.

---

### Zone Selection Principle

No zone is inherently wrong. Each zone works when it matches the emotional context your audience is in when they see your ad and think about their problem.

The smartest ad in the world will fail if it speaks the wrong emotional language.

## Module 4: Diagnosing Your Ad

### The Four Diagnostic Questions

Work through these in order to locate your ad on the valence-intensity map:

**Question 1:** Is the emotion in this ad positive or negative?
- Identify the dominant feeling the ad creates
- Place it on the valence axis (top half or bottom half)

**Question 2:** What's the emotional intensity level?
- Is the ad soft/gentle or urgent/charged?
- Place it on the intensity axis (left side or right side)

**Question 3:** What's the customer's emotional state when seeing this ad?
- Are they distracted (dopamine scrolling)?
- Are they actively thinking about their problem?
- Are they stressed, relaxed, rushed, or contemplative?
- What environment are they likely in? (work, home, commute)

**Question 4:** What emotional experience should we communicate to motivate action?
- What would make them stop scrolling?
- What would make them click through?

### Common Misfire Patterns

**Misfire 1: Calm ad for an urgent buyer**
- You create: Zone 1 (soothing, reassuring)
- Customer state: Stressed, on deadline, needs quick solutions
- Result: Completely ignored—their brain is hunting for fast answers, not calm ones

**Misfire 2: High-energy ad for luxury product**
- You create: Zone 2 or 4 (intense colors, promotional energy)
- Customer expectation: Sophistication, understated quality
- Result: Feels cheap, damages brand perception
- Real example: Jaguar's highly colorful rebrand felt wrong to customers who valued restrained luxury

**Misfire 3: Under-emotional ad for high-emotion problem**
- You create: Low-intensity zone (1 or 3)
- Customer need: High motivation to overcome inertia
- Result: "Save for later" or scroll past—not enough emotional pull to drive action

### The Key Diagnostic Insight

Sometimes ads underperform not because they're emotionally wrong, but because they're not emotional *enough*. The intensity is too low for customers who need a strong push to overcome their status quo.

## Module 5: Tactics to Shift Emotional Tone

You don't need to scrap your ad. You need to adjust the emotional temperature.

### Tactic 1: Swap the Verbs

**Why it works:** The brain processes your image first, then your largest headline second. Verbs are emotional accelerators—they set the action energy of your message.

**How to execute:**
- Identify the main verbs in your headline
- Replace soft/passive verbs with sharp/definitive ones (or vice versa)
- Keep the core message identical; change only the delivery energy

**Example shift (Zone 1 → Zone 2):**
- Before: "Discover your perfect morning ritual"
- After: "Ignite your morning—starting now"

The message (morning routine improvement) stays the same. The intensity shifts from contemplative to urgent.

**Direction guidance:**
- To increase intensity: Use commanding, immediate, action-forcing verbs
- To decrease intensity: Use inviting, gentle, discovery-oriented verbs

---

### Tactic 2: Adjust the Visuals

**Why it works:** Visual elements communicate intensity before the conscious mind processes copy.

**To INCREASE intensity:**
- Use bolder, more saturated colors (bright pink performs consistently well on Meta)
- Zoom in close on the product (proximity = intensity for humans)
- Use tighter crops on faces
- Increase contrast
- Use dynamic poses/angles

**To DECREASE intensity:**
- Desaturate colors slightly
- Zoom out—show more context, more body, more environment
- Use softer lighting (morning light is particularly calming)
- Incorporate muted backgrounds
- Use relaxed, natural poses

**Psychological principle:** Humans instinctively read close proximity as intense/demanding attention, and distance as safe/comfortable. Use this for visual intensity control.

---

### Tactic 3: Change the Emotional Promise (Most Important)

**Why it works:** This addresses the fundamental question of *which emotion* you're selling, not just how you're delivering it.

**The diagnosis:** Read your ad's copy and visuals. What emotion is it actually selling? Is that the emotion your customer needs to feel to take action?

**Key insight:** Being 10% off on the valence-intensity map can make an ad feel completely wrong. Small misalignments create big disconnects.

**The principle:** Don't run static emotions in a dynamic feed. Design emotional arcs that match customer psychology.

## Module 6: Application Framework

### Step-by-Step Execution Process

**Step 1: Select your ad**
Choose an ad that underperformed—specifically one that seemed like it *should* have worked based on offer, creative quality, and targeting.

**Step 2: Diagnose current zone**
Using the four diagnostic questions from Module 4, identify which zone your ad currently occupies on the valence-intensity map.

**Step 3: Assess customer state**
Ask yourself:
- What emotional state is my customer in when they see this ad?
- Are they distracted? Stressed? Relaxed? Actively problem-solving?
- What's their environment? (Work, home, commute, etc.)
- What emotional state do I *need* them in to take action?

**Step 4: Identify the mismatch**
Compare your ad's current zone to the emotional state your customer needs. The gap between these is your problem.

**Step 5: Choose your shift**
Based on what needs to change:
- **Mismatch is intensity only:** Shift verbs and/or visuals
- **Mismatch is valence only:** Shift the emotional promise
- **Mismatch is both:** Full creative revision using zone prompts

**Step 6: Execute the shift**
- Use Tactic 1 (verbs) for quick intensity adjustments
- Use Tactic 2 (visuals) for medium-effort changes
- Use Tactic 3 (emotional promise) with zone prompts for fundamental repositioning

**Step 7: Test and iterate**
Run the revised creative. If still underperforming, reassess zone placement and shift again. Keep testing until you hit the right quadrant.

### Quick-Reference Decision Tree

```
Is the ad performing?
├── YES → Document what's working for future reference
└── NO → Continue diagnosis

Does the ad feel emotionally "off" even though copy/offer is strong?
├── YES → This is a valence-intensity problem
└── NO → Check other factors (targeting, offer, landing page)

Is the customer in a high-urgency state?
├── YES → You likely need Zone 2 or Zone 4
└── NO → Zone 1 or Zone 3 may be appropriate

Is the customer's relationship to the problem positive or negative?
├── Positive (aspiration, desire) → Zone 1 or Zone 2
└── Negative (pain, fear, frustration) → Zone 3 or Zone 4

Is high intensity or low intensity appropriate for this audience?
├── High (need momentum, action, breakthrough) → Zone 2 or Zone 4
└── Low (need trust, reflection, comfort) → Zone 1 or Zone 3
```

---

### Key Principles Summary

1. **Every ad is an emotion, not just a message.** Treat it accordingly.

2. **Emotional misalignment of even 10% creates scroll-past behavior.** Precision matters.

3. **No zone is wrong.** Each works when matched to customer psychology.

4. **Don't run static emotions in a dynamic feed.** Design emotional arcs intentionally.

5. **Sometimes ads fail because they're not emotional enough,** not because they're emotionally wrong.

6. **Diagnose before you tweak.** Random changes waste budget. Systematic diagnosis finds fixes.

7. **The brain processes image first, headline second.** These must align emotionally.

8. **Never use Zone 4 to manufacture fear.** Connect to real emotional experiences—don't create artificial trauma.
