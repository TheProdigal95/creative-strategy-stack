# TEEP Model Course Playbook
## A Psychology-First Framework for Finding Winning Ads Faster

---

# MODULE 1: Introduction and Core Problem

## Why the TEEP Method Exists

Research shows 85% of ads fail to pass the minimum threshold needed for memory recall. Most ads get filtered out by the brain before they're even consciously processed. The brain is designed to ignore anything that doesn't require immediate attention.

**The core problem:** If people aren't seeing your ad, nothing else matters—not your hook, not your CTA, not your offer. Everything gets skipped.

## The Current Approach (And Why It Fails)

Most advertisers take a scattershot approach:
- Dress up an offer in various ways
- Blast it to every customer segment simultaneously
- Test offers, messaging, heuristics, pricing, creators, and scripts all at once
- Kill ads after a few hours if they don't perform

**Why this fails:**
- You cannot isolate what actually worked
- No data to back up assumptions about why someone clicked
- Impossible to replicate winning concepts
- Budgets burn because lessons aren't extracted from ads
- No clear reason for why an ad was created or what to do next

## What the TEEP Method Solves

The TEEP method structures ad testing around where customers actually are in their buying journey. This allows you to:
- Identify where your messaging is strongest
- Find which part of the customer journey needs more investment
- Open up the funnel strategically
- Stop wasting creative energy and budget
- Build repeatable, learnable testing systems

# MODULE 2: The TEEP Framework Overview

## Origin of the Model

The TEEP model comes from a Google study investigating what happens between a customer deciding to find a solution and actually making a purchase. Google recognized that in an era of abundant choice, traditional linear marketing funnels don't capture actual consumer behavior.

## The Four Phases

### Phase 1: Trigger
Something happens that causes the customer to start thinking about solving a problem. This could be:
- Major life events (bought a house, got married, had a baby)
- Minor moments (hungry, bored, distracted)
- Pain points reaching a threshold ("I can't deal with this anymore")

### Phase 2: Exploration
After the trigger, customers seek options. They're asking "What's out there?" They're:
- Not actively making purchase decisions yet
- Discovering which brands exist
- Understanding what products are available
- Browsing without commitment

### Phase 3: Evaluation
Customers narrow down their options. They're asking "Which one is right for me?" This is where:
- "Us vs. them" comparisons become relevant
- Before/after content works well
- Trust and credibility matter most

### Phase 4: Purchase
The emotional pressure builds until the customer takes action. They're asking "Is now the right time?"

## Critical Insight: The Loop

Customers morph back and forth between Exploration and Evaluation repeatedly until emotional pressure pushes them to purchase. They don't move linearly through a funnel—they loop.

**Key research finding:** Brands can dramatically shift customer preferences by being present during Exploration and Evaluation phases and strategically applying psychological principles to reduce friction.

# MODULE 3: Trigger and Exploration Ads

## Why These Two Are Grouped

Trigger and Exploration are combined here because this is where most ad accounts fail. When an ad account underperforms, it's usually because it's not pulling in enough new eyeballs at the top of the funnel—which is where Trigger and Exploration ads do their work.

These are the gatekeepers of attention. If you lose customers here, the rest of your funnel never gets a turn.

---

## Trigger Ads

### Purpose
Make a distracted brain focus—fast. Trigger ads break patterns and force the brain to notice something different.

### Psychology
The brain is wired to notice:
- Differences
- Things that aren't quite right
- Oddities and anomalies
- Pattern breaks

### What Trigger Ads Must Do
- Connect with a person's identity
- Drive curiosity
- Be relevant
- Create a visceral reaction ("Oh my god," "Why?," "That's weird")
- Make people think "I had no idea that was a thing"

### Goal
Stop the scroll. Get reach. Acquire net-new eyeballs. Period.

**Do not focus on:** Conversions or clicks at this stage. The only objective is maximum reach and engagement.

### Metrics to Track
- **Hook rate:** Did they stop?
- **Hold rate:** Did they watch?
- Engagement metrics

### Gut Check
If the customer would ask themselves "Wait, what is this?"—it's a trigger ad.

---

## Exploration Ads

### Purpose
Turn curiosity into interest. Move someone from "What?" to "Oh, that's interesting."

### Psychology
People are looking for themselves in the ad. Exploration ads must be:
- Relatable
- Novel
- Engaging (funny, informative, surprising)
- Aligned with a specific identity or interest

### What Exploration Ads Must Do
- Explain why someone should give more attention to what you're offering
- Keep the flame alive after the initial spark
- Help customers browse and understand options

### Goal
Get clicks. Educate customers. Improve landing page views. Get people to stay longer and learn more.

### Metrics to Track
- **Click-through rate:** Did they click?
- **Browse behavior:** Did they explore the site/landing page?
- **Time on site:** How long did they stay?
- What actions did they take after clicking?

### Gut Check
If the customer would ask themselves "Ooh, that's interesting—but how?"—it's an exploration ad.

---

## Critical Warning: The Exploration Trap

**Do not keep people in Exploration phase too long.**

Running too many educational ads causes customers to gather information endlessly without moving toward purchase. They browse, collect, compare—but never buy.

A killer trigger ad followed by weak exploration creates classic clickbait: high initial interest, zero follow-through.

**The fix:** Always move people toward the next phase. Don't let them get lost while exploring.

---

## Distinguishing Trigger vs. Exploration Ads

These ads can look very similar. The key difference is the ad's core goal:

| Trigger Ad | Exploration Ad |
|------------|----------------|
| Stop the scroll | Get the click |
| Maximize reach | Educate and engage |
| Spark attention | Build interest |
| "What is this?" | "Tell me more" |

### Common Mistake: Blending Phases

Many marketers blend multiple phases into one ad (trigger + exploration + purchase messaging combined). 

**Why this fails:**
- Customers can only be in one phase at a time
- Meta must decide who to show the ad to
- Ads targeting multiple phases become expensive and ineffective
- The core message gets diluted

**The fix:** Keep ad tests separate. One ad, one phase, one clear message.

---

## Trigger vs. Exploration Ad Examples

### Exploration Ad Indicators
- Discusses product-specific details (money-back guarantee, ingredients, features)
- Provides information beyond the core problem
- Helps customer understand the solution

### Trigger Ad Indicators
- Core message focuses only on the problem/desire
- Headlines speak to people in a specific life moment
- Minimal product detail—maximum attention grab

**Even if an ad contains a discount or offer, categorize it by the core message and headline.** An ad saying "Melt away fat in 90 days" with a 60% off tag is still a trigger ad because the headline targets the trigger phase.

# MODULE 4: Evaluation Ads

## The Transition Point

By now, you've captured attention (Trigger) and built interest (Exploration). In the Evaluation phase, the customer's brain runs filters:
- Does this make sense?
- Do I trust it?
- Is it worth it?

This is where most ads stumble outside of the trigger phase. Customers are leaning in but cautious. Curiosity is high, but trust isn't established yet.

## Psychology of Evaluation

Customers evaluate based on:
- **Risk:** What could go wrong?
- **Clarity:** Do I understand this?
- **Belief alignment:** Does this match what I already believe?

Evaluation is about navigating the customer's existing belief system and aligning your offer with their worldview.

## What Evaluation Ads Must Do

Answer these questions the customer is asking themselves:
- Do I trust this brand?
- Do I understand the offer?
- Is this going to be worth my money and time?

If your ad doesn't pass this mental checklist, the sale dies before it starts.

### Gut Check
If you're trying to prove your worth, prove your value, show authority, demonstrate how many people endorse you—it's an evaluation ad.

## Key Principles

**Simplicity = Trust:** Ads that feel simple to process feel more trustworthy.

**Legitimacy over entertainment:** This phase requires more serious, brand-focused content. You're not trying to make people laugh or get bizarre reactions. You're saying: "We are the brand for you. Here's why you should choose us."

## Metrics to Track

Evaluation shows up in metric relationships, not single metrics:

| Metric Pair | What It Indicates |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Watch time + Duration | Were they captivated? |
| Click + Add to cart | Did interest convert to action? |
| Engagement + Comments | Is trust building? |

**Look for signals of trust-building behavior**, not just stopping or clicking.

---

## Critical Warning: Do Not Oversell

The most common killer for evaluation ads is drowning customers in complexity.

**Symptoms of overselling:**
- Landing pages with too much information
- Ads with excessive social proof stacking
- 17 different trust signals crammed into one ad
- Promising everything to everyone

**The consequence:** Even great trigger and exploration ads get destroyed by one bad evaluation ad. You break trust by trying too hard.

**The mindset:** You're not selling. You're helping them evaluate their options and showing why—based on everything they already know—you're still the best choice.

---

## Strong vs. Weak Evaluation Ads

### Strong Evaluation Ads
- Make the logic airtight
- Show receipts (proof of claims)
- Simplify value into clear terms
- Reduce perceived risk
- Include: money-back guarantees, social proof, authority endorsements, testimonials, before/afters, us-vs-them comparisons

**Do not use urgency or scarcity yet.** That's for the Purchase phase.

### Weak Evaluation Ads
- Over-promise ("This will change your life forever")
- Use hype that can't be delivered
- Stack offers on top of inflated claims
- Create bait-and-switch feelings when the landing page doesn't match

**Broken trust is instant and permanent.** If the ad feels even slightly deceptive, you lose the customer.

---

## Building Evaluation Ads: The Core Question

Before creating evaluation ads, ask yourself:

**Are we providing proof that this purchase is safe, smart, and savvy?**

Not proof that you're a good brand—that relationship should already exist from Exploration. Evaluation is about proof of purchase quality.

# MODULE 5: Purchase Ads

## The Final Phase

You've sparked attention, built interest, and established trust. The moment of truth: Can you get them to buy?

Once trust is built, this phase is straightforward. Purchase ads exist to push people over the line.

## Psychology of Purchase

Customers are asking only two questions:
1. Am I comfortable giving my money?
2. Is now the right time?

Your job: Make answering "yes" to both questions frictionless.

## Behavioral Science Insight

People need both:
- **A push:** Urgency nudges them forward
- **A safety net:** Clarity prevents backing out

**Critical distinction:** You can nudge all you want, but they must decide for themselves that now is the right time. External pressure alone won't work.

## The Most Effective Messaging Angle

Instead of creating artificial urgency, use commitment-based messaging:

> "You've already made the decision to [desired outcome]. This helps you get there."

This is stronger than "Limited time offer!" because it acknowledges their internal commitment and encourages completion of a behavior they've already chosen.

## What Purchase Ads Must Do

Present the offer. That's it.

- Pricing structure
- Discount
- Bundle
- Limited-time availability (if genuine)

### Gut Check
If the ad's primary purpose is presenting an offer or prompting immediate action—it's a purchase ad.

## Metrics to Track

Only hard outcomes matter here:
- **Add to cart → Checkout:** Did they try to give you money?
- **Purchase:** Did they complete the transaction?

Track checkout-level behavior to measure efficiency and identify abandoned cart issues. But the core metrics are add-to-cart and purchase—clear signals of purchase intent.

---

## Critical Warning: Do Not Create Friction

Purchase ads fail when customers encounter surprises:
- Product page doesn't match the ad
- Checkout process is complicated
- Something unexpected appears (hidden fees, different product, etc.)

**Commitment bias works for you here.** Customers who've made it to Purchase have somewhat committed. Your job is to make completing that commitment effortless.

**The rule:** Be extremely clear. Reduce friction at every step.

---

## Strong vs. Weak Purchase Ads

### Strong Purchase Ads
- Strip away all complexity
- Show one clear offer
- Use precise numbers (62% off, not "over 60% off")
- Include per-unit pricing if applicable
- Make the value calculation effortless

**Why precise numbers work:** "62% off" makes the brain think "Why not 60? This must be a real calculation—maybe it's a good time to buy." Precise numbers feel more credible than rounded ones.

### Weak Purchase Ads
- Stack multiple offers ("25% off PLUS free gifts PLUS...")
- Include irrelevant features ("100% vegan" on a discount ad)
- Scatter attention with too many messages
- Use vague or complex messaging
- Lack emotional close or urgency

**Without clear urgency or emotional alignment, customers procrastinate.** Scattered attention kills conversion rates.

---

## Building Purchase Ads: The Core Question

Before creating purchase ads, ask yourself:

**Is the offer clear, simple, and immediately actionable?**

"Immediately actionable" means:
- Whatever is shown in the ad is pre-selected on the landing page
- If the ad shows a blue product, the blue product is selected when they arrive
- Zero additional decisions required

**This level of precision matters** because by this stage, customers are fatigued from processing information about you, your competitors, your industry, and countless other ads. They want someone to tell them exactly where to go.

# MODULE 6: Ad Account Structure and Testing Methodology

## The Core Problem in Creative Strategy

The issue isn't ideation, research, or understanding what customers want. Data is abundant, and AI tools help break it down.

**The real issue is structure.** Most accounts lack systems for:
- Getting actionable data from tests
- Understanding what works at each funnel stage
- Avoiding the chaos of random ad testing

## Foundational Principle

Every test you run should align with one stage of the TEEP model.

Instead of dumping hundreds of ads into one campaign and hoping something works, structure tests by funnel stage so you can identify:
- Where you have too many ads (wasted effort)
- Where you have gaps (missed opportunity)
- What's actually driving results at each phase

---

## Budget Split: The Starting Framework

Begin with this allocation:

| TEEP Stage | Budget % | Purpose |
|------------|----------|---------|
| Trigger | 20% | Bring new eyeballs to top of funnel |
| Exploration | 30% | Educate and engage interested browsers |
| Evaluation | 30% | Build trust and prove value |
| Purchase | 20% | Convert with clear offers |

**This is a starting point.** Adjust based on where your funnel is underperforming:
- Not enough new prospects? Increase Trigger
- High traffic but low trust? Increase Evaluation
- Trust but no sales? Increase Purchase

Your optimal split depends on your specific funnel dynamics.

---

## Ad Account Structure: Starter Setup (Lower Budgets)

For accounts spending approximately $3K–$10K/month or just beginning to scale:

### Structure
```
Campaign (One campaign with unified objective)
├── Ad Set: Trigger Ads
│   └── 3-5 ads
├── Ad Set: Exploration Ads
│   └── 3-5 ads
├── Ad Set: Evaluation Ads
│   └── 3-5 ads
└── Ad Set: Purchase Ads
    └── 3-5 ads
```

### Campaign Objective
Define a clear, specific goal. Not "conversions" in the platform sense—a business objective like:
- "Convert women who want to lose belly fat"
- "Acquire homeowners looking for solar solutions"

### Ad Set Organization
Each TEEP stage gets its own ad set with 3-5 ads.

### Format Diversity Within Each Ad Set
Each ad set should include a range of formats to reach different audience preferences:
- 1 video
- 1 GIF
- 1 carousel
- 2 statics

This same format roster applies to every ad set.

**Why format diversity matters:** Different people engage with different formats. Videos appeal to some; statics appeal to others. Cover the range.

### Note on Budget Distribution
Yes, Meta will push spend toward purchase ads (closest to conversion). This structure works well for smaller budgets because it concentrates spend while still covering all funnel stages.

Use ABO (Ad Set Budget Optimization) to manually adjust spend allocation as you learn.

---

## Ad Account Structure: Scaled Setup (Larger Budgets)

When you're scaling and seeing strong results across the account:

### Structure
```
Campaign: Trigger
├── Ad Set: Product A Trigger Ads
├── Ad Set: Product B Trigger Ads
└── Ad Set: Product C Trigger Ads

Campaign: Exploration
├── Ad Set: Product A Exploration Ads
├── Ad Set: Product B Exploration Ads
└── Ad Set: Product C Exploration Ads

Campaign: Evaluation
├── Ad Set: Product A Evaluation Ads
├── Ad Set: Product B Evaluation Ads
└── Ad Set: Product C Evaluation Ads

Campaign: Purchase
├── Ad Set: Product A Purchase Ads
├── Ad Set: Product B Purchase Ads
└── Ad Set: Product C Purchase Ads
```

### Benefit of This Structure
Breaking campaigns by TEEP stage allows testing at the ad set level. You can:
- Test trigger ads by product line
- Test exploration ads by audience segment
- Test evaluation ads by proof type
- Test purchase ads by offer structure

This granularity is only possible (and useful) with sufficient budget to get meaningful data at each level.

---

## Testing Best Practices

### Rule 1: Isolate Variables
Do not test everything at once. Decide what you're testing:
- Headlines only?
- Colors only?
- Creators only?
- Scripts only?

Test toward one learning objective at a time.

### Rule 2: Keep Volume Manageable
3-5 ads per test is sufficient. More than that creates noise without additional insight.

### Rule 3: Keep Budgets Flat
Don't inflate budgets when results stagnate or panic-adjust when something dips. Volatile spending produces volatile data.

**Patience is critical.** Wait 4-7 days before making decisions. Ads dip and recover based on external factors (weather, news cycles, seasonal behavior).

Only restructure or replace ads when you see sustained underperformance across an entire ad set.

### Rule 4: Tag Everything
Maintain rigorous naming conventions and asset organization:
- Name ads by TEEP stage
- Tag in asset folders
- Document what each ad is testing

If you can't quickly identify what an ad was designed to test, your learnings are lost.

---

## Tracking: Account Health Over Individual Ad Performance

### The Mindset Shift
Stop obsessing over making every ad a winner. Focus on whether the overall account is healthy and driving revenue.

**"All boats rise" thinking:**
- Some ads won't convert but will generate massive reach
- Some ads won't get clicks but will drive engagement and comments
- Both types contribute to account health

### How to Track
1. Identify the primary metric for each TEEP stage
2. Benchmark performance by stage
3. Track religiously over time

**You're running a science lab.** Meta is volatile. Things will fluctuate. The long game wins.

### The Principle
"Do your research like a scientist, but make decisions like a gambler."

You're front-loading systematic work so that when opportunities arise, you can capitalize quickly. Play the long game.

# Quick Reference: TEEP Stage Summary

| Stage | Customer Question | Ad Goal | Key Metrics | Don't Do This |
|-------|------------------|---------|-------------|---------------|
| **Trigger** | "What is this?" | Stop scroll, get reach | Hook rate, hold rate | Focus on conversions |
| **Exploration** | "Tell me more" | Get clicks, educate | CTR, browse behavior, time on site | Keep them here too long |
| **Evaluation** | "Can I trust this?" | Build trust, prove value | Watch time + adds to cart, engagement + comments | Oversell or over-complicate |
| **Purchase** | "Is now the time?" | Convert with clear offer | Add to cart, purchase | Create friction or surprise |

---

# Key Warnings Summary

1. **Don't blend TEEP stages in one ad.** One ad, one stage, one clear message.

2. **Don't keep people in Exploration too long.** Educational content that doesn't move people forward creates information hoarders, not buyers.

3. **Don't oversell in Evaluation.** Complexity and hype destroy trust instantly.

4. **Don't create friction at Purchase.** Surprises, mismatched landing pages, or complicated checkout flows kill conversions.

5. **Don't test everything at once.** Isolate variables to get actionable learnings.

6. **Don't panic-adjust budgets.** Wait 4-7 days before making changes.

7. **Don't ignore format diversity.** Cover video, GIF, carousel, and static in each ad set.

8. **Don't forget to tag.** Lost naming conventions mean lost learnings.

# Implementation Checklist

## Initial Setup
- [ ] Define campaign objective (specific business goal, not platform setting)
- [ ] Create 4 ad sets (one per TEEP stage)
- [ ] Develop 3-5 ads per stage with format diversity
- [ ] Establish naming convention and tagging system
- [ ] Set initial budget split (20/30/30/20)

## Ongoing Management
- [ ] Track metrics by TEEP stage
- [ ] Wait 4-7 days before budget adjustments
- [ ] Isolate one variable per test round
- [ ] Replace fatigued ads within their TEEP category
- [ ] Adjust budget split based on funnel performance data

## Scaling Triggers
- [ ] Account showing consistent performance across all stages
- [ ] Clear data on which stages need more investment
- [ ] Budget sufficient to split campaigns by TEEP stage
- [ ] Ready to test at product/segment level within stages
