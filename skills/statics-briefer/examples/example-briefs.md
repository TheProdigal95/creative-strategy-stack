## Example 1: Trigger Stage Brief

BRIEF 1: BACON NECK REALITY CHECK

STRATEGY NOTES
- ANGLE: Bacon neck collar fail
- STAGE: Trigger
- STAGE GOAL: Stop-scroll recognition—make it feel like "that's us" instantly

EMOTIONAL JOURNEY:
- Customer current state: Zone 3 (quietly frustrated, resigned to collar issues)
- Target state: Zone 1 (relieved, validated, hopeful solution exists)
- Ad positioning: Zone 3 (meets them at frustration, uses their language)
- Journey rationale: We acknowledge the problem with their exact language ("bacon neck") which creates instant recognition and validation. The headline doesn't dwell in frustration—it hints at solution, moving them from "this is normal" to "oh, this doesn't have to be normal."

- SELF TARGET: Actual
- SELF RATIONALE: Validates that their frustration with collars is legitimate, not excessive standards. Gives permission to be annoyed.
- EMOTIONAL MOVE: Quietly annoyed → Validated and relieved

---

```
CONTEXT

The customer is scrolling, half-paying attention. This ad needs to create instant recognition: "That's exactly what happens to my shirts." We're naming the problem they've accepted as normal—bacon-shaped collars after a few washes—and making them realize it's not inevitable.


FORMAT

Problem creative (close-up visual)

Single dominant image showing the collar problem, minimal text overlay, direct headline.


ELEMENTS

- Close-up of wavy bacon-shaped collar
- Brand logo (corner)
- CTA button
- Optional: "Sound familiar?" callout near collar


FOR DESIGNER: PHASE 1 (Image Generation)

IMAGE IDEAS:

Option A: Bathroom mirror moment
Close-up of person adjusting a black tee collar in mirror, collar visibly wavy/deformed. Morning routine setting. Natural lighting.
Why it works: Relatable daily moment. Shows the problem in context of real use.

Option B: Laundry basket overhead shot
Black tees piled in basket, all with visibly wavy collars. Shot from above.
Why it works: Shows this isn't one bad shirt—it's a pattern. Accumulation implies repeated disappointment.

Option C: Two tees side-by-side on hanger
One fresh from store (straight collar), one after washes (bacon collar). Stark comparison.
Why it works: Before/after without requiring explanation. Visual proof of degradation.


FOR DESIGNER: PHASE 2 (Final Composition)

COMPOSITION:
- Collar problem must be the dominant visual element (60%+ of ad space)
- Headline overlaid or directly above collar image
- CTA bottom right, secondary to the visual
- Minimal white space—fill the frame with the problem

COLOR GUIDANCE:
- Muted, relatable tones (not dramatic or alarming)
- Black tee on neutral background
- Avoid high contrast—this is subtle frustration, not crisis

WHAT TO AVOID:
- Wide environmental shots that minimize the collar issue
- Multiple problems shown at once (stay focused on collar)
- Dramatic lighting or styling (this is everyday annoyance, not tragedy)
- Stock photo aesthetics (needs to feel real, not staged)


COPY

HEADLINES:
- You're not picky—bacon neck ruins every tee.
- Your collar turns into bacon. Not you.
- Bacon neck collars are not inevitable.

SUBHEADING:
Hemp holds its shape. Cotton doesn't.

CTA:
See the difference
```

## Example 2: Exploration Stage Brief

BRIEF 2: THE PFAS TRUTH

STRATEGY NOTES
- ANGLE: Zero PFAS/microplastics
- STAGE: Exploration
- STAGE GOAL: Build curiosity about mechanism—make them want to understand how it works

EMOTIONAL JOURNEY:
- Customer current state: Zone 3 (vaguely concerned about "chemicals in clothes," confused about PFAS)
- Target state: Zone 4 (informed urgency, motivated to act)
- Ad positioning: Zone 4 (educational alarm—makes invisible threat visible)
- Journey rationale: Customer has passive concern but no understanding. The side-by-side comparison makes the invisible contamination visible (microscope views, water murky with microplastics). This creates Zone 4 urgency: "This is happening to me right now." We move them from vague worry to informed action.

- SELF TARGET: Ought
- SELF RATIONALE: Creates obligation to protect their health. Frames continued wearing of PFAS fabrics as failing a responsibility to themselves.
- EMOTIONAL MOVE: Vaguely concerned → Informed urgency

---

```
CONTEXT

The customer knows "chemicals in clothes" is bad but doesn't understand what PFAS actually does or why it matters. This ad educates on the mechanism: PFAS makes fabrics moisture-wicking but contaminates skin and water. Natural fiber engineering performs without synthetic coatings. We're moving them from passive concern to active understanding.


FORMAT

Side-by-side comparison (educational breakdown)

LEFT COLUMN (The Problem):
- Synthetic fabric under microscope
- PFAS coating visual
- Particle shedding illustration
- "Most 'performance' tees" label

RIGHT COLUMN (The Solution):
- Natural fiber weave closeup
- Zero chemical coating
- Clean fiber structure
- "Hemp performance" label


ELEMENTS

- Split-screen microscope imagery (synthetic vs. natural)
- "Zero PFAS" badge
- "Lab-tested" callout
- CTA button
- Optional: "Tested by [third-party lab]" trust signal


FOR DESIGNER: PHASE 1 (Image Generation)

IMAGE IDEAS:

Option A: Microscope view split-screen
Left: synthetic fabric fibers with visible particles/coating shedding. Right: clean hemp fiber weave, no particles.
Why it works: Shows the invisible problem made visible. Scientific credibility without requiring chemistry knowledge.

Option B: Washing machine water comparison
Two beakers or jars of water—left murky with microplastics from synthetic wash, right clear from hemp wash.
Why it works: Tangible proof of contamination. Visual you can't un-see.

Option C: Skin absorption diagram
Simple illustration showing PFAS particles passing through skin from fabric contact.
Why it works: Makes the personal health risk concrete and immediate. "This is happening to you."


FOR DESIGNER: PHASE 2 (Final Composition)

COMPOSITION:
- Equal visual weight to both sides of comparison (50/50 split)
- Clear labeling: "Synthetic" vs. "Hemp" or "PFAS" vs. "Zero PFAS"
- Educational tone, not alarmist (informative urgency)
- CTA should invite learning: "See the test results" not "Buy now"

COLOR GUIDANCE:
- Clean, clinical palette (whites, light grays, blue accents)
- Avoid overly saturated colors (we want credibility, not drama)
- Left side (problem) can be slightly desaturated/cooler to imply contamination

WHAT TO AVOID:
- Overly technical language or chemical formulas
- Alarmist imagery (skulls, warning symbols, red alerts)
- Cluttered comparison—keep it to 2-3 key differences max
- Making it feel like a science textbook (approachable education, not lecture)


COPY

HEADLINES:
- Most performance tees shed PFAS. Hemp doesn't.
- Don't wear chemicals your body absorbs.
- Zero PFAS. Zero microplastics. Actual performance.

SUBHEADING:
Lab-tested: No forever chemicals, no plastic shedding.

CTA:
See the test results
```

## Example 3: Evaluation Stage Brief

BRIEF 3: THE LIFETIME PROMISE

STRATEGY NOTES
- ANGLE: Lifetime guarantee
- STAGE: Evaluation
- STAGE GOAL: Address objections and build confidence—make them feel this is the right choice

EMOTIONAL JOURNEY:
- Customer current state: Zone 3 (skeptical, hesitant—"been burned before by quality claims")
- Target state: Zone 1 (confident, assured, ready to trust)
- Ad positioning: Zone 1 (calm confidence—removes risk without hype)
- Journey rationale: Customer is skeptical because past "premium" products failed. We meet this skepticism by acknowledging it ("You've been burned before. We get it.") then provide the ultimate risk-remover: lifetime guarantee. Zone 1's calm, understated tone builds trust better than hype. We move them from "what if it breaks?" to "oh, if it breaks they'll replace it forever."

- SELF TARGET: Actual
- SELF RATIONALE: Validates past disappointments with "quality" claims. Acknowledges their skepticism is earned, not cynical.
- EMOTIONAL MOVE: Hesitant and skeptical → Confident and assured

---

```
CONTEXT

The customer is considering the purchase but hesitating: "What if it doesn't last? What if I'm the unlucky one?" This ad removes that risk. We've engineered these to last decades, but if yours doesn't, we replace it free. Companies that offer lifetime guarantees build things that don't need them. The message: buy once, own forever.


FORMAT

CTA-focused (minimal distraction)

Single product image (hero shot) + guarantee copy + clear CTA. No competing elements. Pure confidence.


ELEMENTS

- Single tee hero image (hanging or folded, timeless aesthetic)
- "Lifetime Guarantee" badge (prominent)
- Guarantee terms (simple, 1-2 lines)
- CTA button
- Optional: "No questions asked" callout


FOR DESIGNER: PHASE 1 (Image Generation)

IMAGE IDEAS:

Option A: Timeless closet moment
Single hemp tee hanging in minimal closet, soft natural light through window. Calm, enduring aesthetic.
Why it works: Implies permanence and longevity. This is the last black tee you'll need.

Option B: Product on clean surface
Tee folded perfectly on wooden table or white surface. Clean, confident product shot.
Why it works: Classic product photography. Lets the guarantee do the talking without visual distraction.

Option C: Calendar flipping through years
Tee remains constant while background shows passage of time (calendar pages, seasons changing).
Why it works: Visualizes "lifetime" concept. Shows durability over time without literal wear-and-tear imagery.


FOR DESIGNER: PHASE 2 (Final Composition)

COMPOSITION:
- Product image as focal point (centered or slightly off-center)
- Ample breathing room (white space conveys confidence, not clutter)
- Guarantee badge near product, not competing for attention
- Headline above or to the side, supporting not dominating
- CTA bottom center, clear and unobscured

COLOR GUIDANCE:
- Calm, confident palette (soft neutrals, natural wood tones, subtle greens)
- Avoid urgency colors (reds, oranges)
- Light, airy feel—not heavy or dramatic

WHAT TO AVOID:
- Busy backgrounds or competing visual elements
- Urgency tactics (timers, countdown, "limited stock")
- Overly designed or stylized product shots (straightforward beats clever here)
- Making the guarantee text too long or legalistic (simple promise, not contract)


COPY

HEADLINES:
- If it fails, we replace it. Forever.
- You've been burned before. We get it.
- Buy it once. Own it forever.

SUBHEADING:
Lifetime guarantee. No timeline. No questions.

CTA:
Read the guarantee
```

## Example 4: Purchase Stage Brief

BRIEF 4: THE GOLD BAR TEE

STRATEGY NOTES
- ANGLE: Gold bar surprise (1 in 100 orders)
- STAGE: Purchase
- STAGE GOAL: Remove friction and create urgency—make them act now

EMOTIONAL JOURNEY:
- Customer current state: Zone 1 (interested, on the fence, no urgency)
- Target state: Zone 2 (excited, energized, "I want to be the one")
- Ad positioning: Zone 2 (pure excitement—aspirational lottery energy)
- Journey rationale: Customer is interested but procrastinating. The gold bar surprise creates Zone 2 excitement: "What if it's me?" This isn't about the gold bar value—it's about the thrill of possibility. We move them from passive interest to active desire with "next order could be yours" energy.

- SELF TARGET: Ideal
- SELF RATIONALE: Aspirational identity framing—people who take action, who seize opportunities. Positions ordering as aligned with who they want to be.
- EMOTIONAL MOVE: Curious → Excited and ready to act

---

```
CONTEXT

The customer is on the edge of purchase. This ad creates immediate action energy: "One in 100 orders gets a 1oz gold bar—worth more than the shirt." No minimum purchase, no tricks. Every order is a chance. The excitement isn't about the gold bar (though that's fun)—it's about the delight of "what if it's me?"


FORMAT

Offer stack (excitement-focused)

Main offer (the tee) + surprise element (gold bar) + social proof ("1 in 100") + urgency cue (implicit: next order could be yours).


ELEMENTS

- Gold bar image (shiny, tangible, real)
- Tee product image (secondary to gold bar)
- "1 in 100 orders" callout (prominent)
- CTA button (action-oriented)
- Optional: Unboxing imagery or customer reaction


FOR DESIGNER: PHASE 1 (Image Generation)

IMAGE IDEAS:

Option A: Unboxing surprise moment
Hands opening black package, gold bar visible in tissue paper, genuine surprise/delight expression.
Why it works: Captures the "holy shit" moment. Emotional peak of discovery. Makes it feel real.

Option B: Gold bar + tee side-by-side
1oz gold bar next to folded tee on clean surface. "One of these comes free" implication.
Why it works: Direct visual comparison. Shows the value mismatch (gold bar worth more than tee). Simple, clear.

Option C: Pure gold bar closeup
Tight crop on 1oz gold bar with "UndrDog" or brand stamp visible. Nothing else.
Why it works: Maximum focus on the prize. Lets headline do the work. Gold is inherently attention-grabbing.


FOR DESIGNER: PHASE 2 (Final Composition)

COMPOSITION:
- Gold bar should be visually dominant (this is the hook)
- "1 in 100" stat needs high visibility (top corner or overlay)
- Headline energetic and prominent
- CTA large and action-oriented ("Complete your order" not "Learn more")
- Minimal friction—every element points toward checkout

COLOR GUIDANCE:
- Bold, energetic palette (golds, blacks, high contrast)
- Make gold bar shine (lighting/highlights)
- Avoid muted tones—this is Zone 2, high energy

WHAT TO AVOID:
- Making the gold bar look fake or CGI (use real product imagery)
- Burying the "1 in 100" stat in fine print
- Passive CTAs that don't create urgency ("See details" vs. "Order now")
- Cluttering with too many terms/conditions (keep it simple: 1 in 100, that's it)


COPY

HEADLINES:
- 1 in 100 orders gets a free gold bar.
- Win a gold bar. Order a tee.
- Next order could be yours. 1oz gold bar inside.

SUBHEADING:
Every order is a chance. No minimum. No tricks.

CTA:
Complete your order
```

## Quality Check Examples

### ✅ PASSES 2-Second Zero-Context Test (Ad-Level Clarity)

**Important:** The clarity test applies to the complete ad (format + visual + headline), not just the headline alone.

**Example 1: Strong visual context**
- Format: Product hero shot - t-shirt on person
- Headline: "The Man Boob Destroyer"
- Result: ✅ CLEAR - Visual makes product obvious, headline can be witty

**Example 2: Educational format**
- Format: Side-by-side comparison (microscope views)
- Headline: "Most performance tees shed PFAS. Hemp doesn't."
- Result: ✅ CLEAR - Headline explicitly mentions product because visual is conceptual

**Example 3: Weak visual + vague headline**
- Format: Generic gym background
- Headline: "Does he lift?"
- Result: ❌ UNCLEAR - What am I buying? The visual doesn't show product, headline doesn't mention it

---

### ✅ VoC + Clarity Balance

Using customer language improves resonance, but must maintain ad-level clarity.

**VoC without adequate clarity:**
- "Does he lift?" (Their words, but combined with weak visual = unclear product)
- "Man boob problems?" (Calls out issue, no solution mentioned, no product shown)

**VoC with ad-level clarity:**
- "Get The 'Does He Lift?' T-Shirt" (VoC + explicit product name) ✅
- "The Man Boob Destroyer" + t-shirt photo (VoC + visual clarity) ✅
- "Man Boobs? This Shirt Hides Them" (VoC + product + benefit) ✅

---

### ✅ Headlines Follow Clarity + Brevity

**Good examples (3-8 words, clear about offering):**
- "If it fails, we replace it. Forever." (7 words) — Clear offer + product implication
- "1 in 100 orders gets a free gold bar." (9 words) — Specific numbers + clear offer
- "Zero PFAS. Zero microplastics. Actual performance." (5 words) — Product attributes + clear benefit

**Bad examples:**
- "Choose better. Feel better." — Vague, no product mention
- "The future of fabric." — Abstract, no clear offer
- "Innovation meets comfort." — Marketing speak, not direct-response

---

### ✅ Format Strength Affects Headline Freedom

**When format includes prominent product imagery:**
- Headlines can be wittier, more conceptual
- Visual provides clarity
- Example: "The Man Boob Destroyer" works with t-shirt hero shot

**When format is educational/conceptual:**
- Headlines should be more explicit
- Visual doesn't show product clearly
- Example: "Most performance tees shed PFAS. Hemp doesn't." needed with microscope comparison

**Universal products (t-shirts, coffee, shoes):**
- Can rely more on visual, headline can be wittier

**Niche products (specialized gear, technical supplements):**
- Headline should help clarify what it is

---

### ✅ Phase 1 vs. Phase 2 Separation

**Phase 1 (Image Generation):**
- "Unboxing surprise moment: Hands opening black package, gold bar visible in tissue paper"
- Describes WHAT the image depicts
- Ready to adapt into AI image generation prompt

**Phase 2 (Final Composition):**
- "Gold bar should be visually dominant, '1 in 100' stat needs high visibility, CTA large and action-oriented"
- Describes HOW to arrange elements in final ad
- Human designer work after image is generated

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't put layout instructions in Phase 1 ("gold bar top left") — that's Phase 2
- Don't put AI styling in Phase 2 ("high contrast lighting") — AI determines that in Phase 1

## Format Structure Reference

**OUTSIDE copy block (for strategists/reviewers):**
```
BRIEF [NUMBER]: [TITLE]

STRATEGY NOTES
- ANGLE:
- STAGE:
- STAGE GOAL:

EMOTIONAL JOURNEY:
- Customer current state:
- Target state:
- Ad positioning:
- Journey rationale:

- SELF TARGET:
- SELF RATIONALE:
- EMOTIONAL MOVE:

---
```

**INSIDE copy block (for designer execution):**
- CONTEXT (2-3 sentences)
- FORMAT (visual structure + column/card details if applicable)
- ELEMENTS (bullet list)
- FOR DESIGNER: PHASE 1 (3 image ideas)
- FOR DESIGNER: PHASE 2 (composition, color, what to avoid)
- COPY (headlines, subheading, CTA)

This separation ensures strategists get framework context, designers get actionable direction, and everything execution-related is copy-able in one block.
