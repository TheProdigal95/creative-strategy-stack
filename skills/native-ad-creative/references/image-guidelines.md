# Image Guidelines

The image's only job is to bypass the brain's ad filter. If it looks like an ad, you've already lost.

---

## Core Principle: Ugly Wins

Polish kills native ads. Rawness prints.

Your creative team would reject these images in seconds. That's exactly why they work. Native ad images should look like:

- A Facebook post from 2012
- A local news thumbnail
- Something your aunt would share
- A phone photo someone took on their way to work
- An article illustration from a blog
- A recipe post
- Content someone's neighbor would forward

They should **never** look like:
- A brand campaign
- A stock photo from a paid library (obvious ones)
- Anything with a logo, brand colors, or design system
- A professional photoshoot
- Anything with text overlays that look designed

---

## What The Brain Should File It As

Every image should pass this test: **What does the reader's brain categorize this as?**

Good answers:
- "That's a news article about my area"
- "That's a recipe or food post"
- "That's someone sharing their results"
- "That's a health article illustration"
- "That's a social media post from a real person"

Bad answers:
- "That's an ad"
- "That's a brand trying to sell me something"
- "That's too polished to be real"

## Image Types That Work

### The Phone Photo
A picture that looks like someone took it with their phone. Real environment. Zero polish. A supplement bottle on a kitchen counter with reading glasses next to it. A person standing in line at the DMV. Someone stretching in their living room.

**Why:** Feels authentic. Feels like someone's real life. The ad filter never activates.

### The Editorial Illustration
Pencil sketches, simple diagrams, medical-style illustrations that look like they belong in an article. A sketch of a torso with red areas highlighted. A simple diagram of a joint.

**Why:** Looks like educational content, not marketing. The brain categorizes it as information.

### The Before/After (Raw)
Side-by-side photos that look like someone sent them to their friend. Not professionally lit. Not retouched. Zoomed in on the actual problem area.

**Why:** It's proof. Raw and believable. It doesn't look like marketing — it looks like a result someone texted to their mum.

### The Bizarre/Unexpected
An angry old man photoshopped next to eggs. A 1950s woman looking at a pelvis diagram. A photo of bananas next to a diabetes headline. Deliberately weird. Deliberately memorable.

**Why:** Pattern interrupt. The image doesn't match expectations, so the brain flags it as novel. Novel = worth investigating. The scroll stops.

### The Authority Figure
A doctor, specialist, or professional — but not a stock photo doctor. Think: someone who looks like they could actually be your doctor. Real, imperfect, human.

**Why:** Borrowed authority from the image itself. When combined with "Specialist Says" headlines, the image pre-sells the credibility.

### The Scene/Location
A DMV line. A kitchen counter. An attic. Real places that signal "this is about my life." A cardboard sign in an attic that says "1000s of homeowners" looks like a local news story.

**Why:** Location grounds the ad in reality. It stops being abstract marketing and becomes "something happening near me."

## Image Pairing Rules

### The image must match the native frame
If the headline sounds like news, the image should look like a news thumbnail. If the headline sounds like a health article, the image should look like an editorial illustration. If the headline sounds like a social post, the image should look like a phone photo.

### The image should pre-sell the positioning
An older man doing a simple stretch implies the solution is natural and easy — not pharmaceutical. A supplement bottle on a kitchen counter implies an everyday product, not a medical intervention. The image sets expectations before the click.

### The image can be the curiosity gap
Sometimes the image IS the hook. A bizarre pairing (angry man + eggs, bananas + diabetes) creates a visual curiosity gap that works with the headline to create an even stronger open loop.

### Never resolve the promise visually
Don't show the product working. Don't show the happy outcome. The image should raise questions, not answer them. Show the problem, the setup, the authority — never the resolution.

## Image Direction Format

When describing image direction for an ad concept, include:

1. **Scene:** What's literally in the image
2. **Style:** How it looks (phone photo, editorial, sketch, etc.)
3. **Mood:** What feeling it creates
4. **Native frame:** What the brain categorizes it as (news, social post, article, etc.)
5. **Why this pairing:** How it works with the specific headline

**Example:**

> **Scene:** Close-up of an open supplement bottle on a kitchen counter. Reading glasses and a handwritten checklist beside it. Natural light from a window.
> **Style:** Phone photo. Real environment. Zero polish. Slight blur acceptable.
> **Mood:** Everyday, authentic, like someone documenting their morning routine.
> **Native frame:** Looks like a product review someone posted to Facebook, not a brand ad.
> **Why this pairing:** "We Tested the Joint Supplement Going Viral With Seniors" needs an image that reinforces the editorial/review angle. A kitchen counter photo says "a real person tried this at home" — it validates the third-party investigation framing without looking commercial.

---

## The Rule

Skip the 30-year-old fitness model. Your buyer is 55+, in pain, and has been burned before. They trust what looks real over what looks expensive.

Make it feel like their neighbor tried it and reported back.
