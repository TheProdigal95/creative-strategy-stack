# Ad Breakdowns

Real native ads dissected. Each breakdown shows how headline, image, and psychology work together. Use these as reference when building ad concepts.

---

## 1. The Gelatin Ad

**Headline:** "People Are Turning To Gelatin For Stubborn Belly Fat"

**Image:** Looks like food content. A recipe. Something organic and editorial. The ad filter in the brain never activates.

**Breakdown:**

- **Social Proof:** "People Are Turning To" — not "try this" or "you should use." Other people are already doing this. You're not being sold. You're being shown a trend you're late to.
- **Universal Pain + Qualifier:** "Stubborn Belly Fat" — not just belly fat. Stubborn belly fat. The kind you've tried to lose. The kind that won't go.
- **Discovery Angle:** Gelatin. Not a pill. Not a program. A weird, soft, jiggly food you forgot existed. The novelty breaks the pattern. The brain flags it as new information, not advertising.
- **Curiosity Gap:** Why gelatin? How does it work? The headline gives the WHAT but not the HOW.

**Lesson:** "People are turning to X" beats "You should try X." Make them feel like they're discovering a movement, not being pitched a product.

## 2. The DMV Ad

**Headline:** "Senior Drivers In Idaho Did Not See This Coming"

**Image:** A photo of people standing in line at the DMV. No product. No benefit visual. Nothing. Just a DMV line.

**Breakdown:**

- **Identity Filtering:** "Senior Drivers In Idaho" — disqualifies 99% of scrollers. If you're a senior driver in Idaho, this ad just called your name in a crowded room.
- **Fear + Urgency:** "Did Not See This Coming" — a threat wrapped in curiosity. Something changed. Something bad. And you missed it. Loss aversion takes over instantly.
- **Curiosity Gap:** New law? Rate hike? License requirement? A closed door with smoke coming out from under it. You must open it.
- **Native Framing:** The DMV image makes this look like local news. Not an ad. The brain categorizes it as information.

**Lesson:** Specificity is a weapon. Speak to one person. Scare them a little. Leave the loop open.

## 3. The Insulation Scheme Ad

**Headline:** "British Homeowners Should Take Advantage of This Insulation Scheme"

**Image:** Stock photo of a guy in an attic. Holding insulation. With a cardboard sign that looks like it was made for a protest reading "1000s of homeowners." No branding. No sleek visuals. Looks like a local news thumbnail.

**Breakdown:**

- **Exclusivity Framing:** "British Homeowners" — immediate tribal qualification. If you're a British homeowner, this is for you.
- **FOMO:** "Should Take Advantage" — there's something on the table you're not grabbing. A scheme. A loophole. Others are already getting it. You're being left behind.
- **Third-Party Legitimacy:** "Scheme" is UK-speak for government program. Feels like a PSA, not an ad. Borrows government authority without mentioning the brand.
- **Curiosity Gap:** What scheme? How do I qualify? How much do I save? The headline gives nothing but the existence of an opportunity.

**Lesson:** Make it feel like news, not an ad. If it looks like something your local newspaper would post, the guard drops. Trust beats polish.

## 4. The Eggs Ad

**Headline:** "Anyone Over 60: Eggs Are A Risk Hiding In Plain Sight"

**Image:** An angry old man photoshopped next to some eggs. Looks like a fever dream. Like someone's first day using Canva.

**Breakdown:**

- **Enemy Framing:** Eggs — the breakfast staple you've eaten your whole life. Now it's a threat. That betrayal triggers emotion.
- **Age-Based Targeting:** "Anyone Over 60" — if you're in that demo, this ad grabbed you by the collar. It's for you. And there's a risk.
- **Fear + Discovery:** "Hiding In Plain Sight" — you've been duped. Something this common, this trusted, is actually dangerous. Pattern interrupt that overrides logic.
- **Curiosity Gap:** What's the risk? Cholesterol? Something new? The headline refuses to say.

**Lesson:** Safe + familiar + "actually dangerous" = unstoppable click formula. Nobody needs another health tip. Everyone clicks on "the thing you eat daily is silently hurting you."

## 5. The Collagen Ad

**Headline:** "Plastic Surgeon Reveals Why Collagen Is Making Your Wrinkles Worse"

**Image:** Raw before/after. Zoomed in on forehead wrinkles and crow's feet. Not fancy — looks like a result someone sent their mum.

**Breakdown:**

- **Third-Party Authority:** "Plastic Surgeon Reveals" — instant credibility hijack. An expert is exposing something. Trust and conspiracy brain fire simultaneously.
- **Enemy Framing:** Collagen is the villain. The thing you've been buying. The thing "they" told you works. Now you feel smart for questioning it.
- **Curiosity Gap:** "Household Item" — what item? Kitchen? Bathroom? The brain will not rest.
- **High Initial Pain:** Before image zoomed in on the insecurity. Makes the viewer feel uncomfortable in 0.5 seconds.

**Lesson:** Credibility + curiosity + pain = clicks. You don't need a design team. You need a headline that makes the scroll stop and the brain itch.

## 6. The Prostate Ad

**Headline:** "Prostate Problems Have Nothing To Do With Age"

**Image:** An older man doing a stretch. Implies the solution is natural, simple, not pharmaceutical. Pre-sells the product positioning before the click.

**Breakdown:**

- **Contrarian Framing:** "Has Nothing to Do with Age" — challenges the accepted belief. Every man over 50 has been told this is inevitable. This ad says: wrong. Confusion + curiosity.
- **Discovery Angle:** "This One Common..." implies a hidden daily habit. Reframes from genetics to behavior. Behavior can be changed. That's hope. Hope sells.
- **Curiosity Gap:** Headline is intentionally cut off. "One Common" what? Food? Activity? The brain cannot scroll past an unfinished sentence.
- **Universal Pain:** Waking up 4x a night. Anxiety. Embarrassment. Massive emotional charge.

**Lesson:** Challenge a "known truth" and you own the conversation. Contrarian framing + curiosity + high pain = native ad domination.

## 7. The Liver/Belly Fat Ad

**Headline:** "Cleanse Your Liver To Lose Belly Fat"

**Image:** A pencil sketch with red blobs on a torso. Zero brand guidelines. Looks like an article illustration, not an ad.

**Breakdown:**

- **Enemy Framing:** The liver is the villain. Not you. Not your discipline. Your toxic liver is sabotaging you. Removes shame. Creates an external target.
- **Discovery Angle:** Not another "eat less move more" ad. Feels like insider information the diet industry is hiding.
- **Universal Pain:** Belly fat — the biggest insecurity for the 35-65 demo. High emotional charge = high click-through.
- **Curiosity Gap:** The bizarre connection (liver → belly fat) must be clicked to understand. The brain can't leave the loop open.

**Lesson:** Native ads don't need to look good. They need to look native. Copy sells. Design just gets in the way.

## 8. The Arthritis Doctor Ad

**Headline:** "Rheumatologist Says: Please Stop Eating This Food If You Have Arthritis"

**Image:** A doctor figure — not a stock photo model. Someone who looks like they could be your actual doctor.

**Breakdown:**

- **Authority Play:** "Rheumatologist" isn't just a title. It's instant credibility. You don't question a specialist.
- **Emotional Trigger:** "Please" changes everything. Not "you should." Not "studies show." Please. A doctor is worried about you personally.
- **Identity Filter:** "If you have arthritis" — if that's you, you physically cannot scroll past. If not, you were never the customer.
- **Curiosity Gap:** "This food..." — which food? The one you had for breakfast? The one you're about to eat? You need to know.

**Lesson:** Four elements. One sentence. Stop overcomplicating ads. Borrow trust and create open loops.

## 9. The Blood Pressure Ad

**Headline:** "If You Have High Blood Pressure, Quickly Drink A Glass Of..."

**Image:** A red-faced man about to explode. A visual metaphor of how they feel.

**Breakdown:**

- **Instant Qualification:** "High blood pressure?" — three words and you've filtered for exactly who you want. Keeps CPCs low.
- **Urgency:** "Quickly" — this is for people who need relief today, not a 90-day plan.
- **Unfinished Loop:** "Drink a glass of..." — of what? Water? Juice? The brain demands the answer. The sentence is cut mid-thought.
- **No product, no brand, no clinical study.** Just a visual metaphor of the pain and a sentence they have to finish.

**Lesson:** Let curiosity do the clicking. Explain later. The ad's job is to hook. The advertorial does the selling.

## 10. The Sciatica Specialist Ad

**Headline:** "Spine Specialist Says: Do This For 15 Minutes To Relieve Sciatica"

**Image:** A 1950s-style woman looking at a pelvis diagram. Bizarre. Memorable.

**Breakdown:**

- **Borrowed Authority:** "Spine Specialist Says" — a specialist is giving direct advice. You normally listen to your doctor.
- **Micro-commitment:** "Do this for 15min" — not a lifestyle change. Not a 6-week program. Fifteen minutes. Absolutely doable.
- **Clear Outcome:** "To relieve sciatica" — not "support your back health." Specific pain → specific promise.
- **Retro Image:** The 1950s aesthetic is unexpected. It's not what you see in modern ads. Pattern interrupt through visual anachronism.

**Lesson:** Stop making ads look like ads. Start making them look like advice from an authority. People automatically start listening.

---

## Patterns Across All Breakdowns

1. **Every winning ad uses at least 2 psychological principles stacked together**
2. **The image never looks like an ad** — it looks like news, content, social, or editorial
3. **The uglier the creative, the more native it feels** — polish triggers the ad filter
4. **Specificity always beats broad targeting** — one person reached deeply beats many reached shallowly
5. **The headline does one thing: create an open loop** — it never resolves, never explains, never sells
6. **The image pre-sells the positioning** — it sets expectations about the type of solution before the click
