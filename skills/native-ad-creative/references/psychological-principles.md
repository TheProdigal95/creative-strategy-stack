# Psychological Principles

The mechanisms that make native ads work. Every headline and image should use at least one of these. The strongest ads stack two or three together.

---

## 1. Curiosity Gap (The Zeigarnik Effect)

The brain has a design flaw: it cannot tolerate incomplete patterns. Unfinished tasks create cognitive tension — a mental itch that demands scratching. The brain allocates background processing power to incomplete information until it finds resolution.

This is why a waiter remembers a complicated order perfectly until the bill is paid, then forgets it entirely. The task was "open." Once closed, the brain releases it.

**How to use it:**

Give the reader the WHAT but not the HOW. Open a loop that can only be closed by clicking.

- Name the problem but not the solution: "The breakfast habit cardiologists are warning about"
- Start a story with no ending: "Doctors Said She Had 6 Months. What She Did Next Changed Everything."
- Make a bizarre connection with no explanation: "People are turning to gelatin for stubborn belly fat"
- Cut the sentence short: "If you have high blood pressure, quickly drink a glass of..."

**The click isn't a choice. It's a compulsion.** The Zeigarnik Effect operates below conscious skepticism. Even when the reader knows it's probably an ad, the open loop overrides that awareness.

**Key rule:** Never resolve the curiosity in the headline. The headline's only job is to create the question. The click answers it.

## 2. Enemy Framing

Turn something trusted into a threat and you own attention.

The formula: safe + familiar + "actually dangerous" = unstoppable click.

**How it works:**

The reader has trusted something their whole life — eggs, breakfast cereal, collagen, age as an explanation. The ad says: that thing is the problem. This creates a sense of betrayal, which triggers emotion. Emotion triggers clicks.

Enemy framing also removes shame. "Your toxic liver is sabotaging you" makes the villain external. It's not your discipline. It's not your fault. There's a hidden enemy.

**Critical rule: One enemy, not a list.** "This one food" gives a single target to blame. Much more powerful than listing 27 causes. The brain loves a single villain. Give them one enemy, then offer the weapon. Multiple enemies dilute the emotion and overwhelm the reader.

**Examples:**
- Eggs reframed as a hidden risk for people over 60
- Collagen reframed as making wrinkles worse
- Age reframed as having nothing to do with prostate problems

**Why it's powerful:** Nobody clicks on "Prostate Problems Are Normal." Everyone clicks on "Everything You Believed Is Wrong."

---

## 3. Authority / Third-Party Legitimacy

Borrow credibility from experts, editorial framing, or institutional language.

**Three forms:**

**Expert authority:** "Rheumatologist Says," "Spine Specialist Reveals," "Plastic Surgeon Warns." You don't question a specialist. You listen. When they say "please" — it goes from advice to personal concern.

**Editorial/investigative framing:** "We Tested the Joint Supplement Going Viral With Seniors." This positions the offer as something investigated, not advertised. The brand isn't talking. A third party is reporting.

**Institutional framing:** Words like "scheme" (UK government program), "benefit," "program" borrow authority from institutions without naming the actual brand. The ad feels like a PSA, not marketing.

**Key insight:** "We tested it" and "a specialist says" hit differently than "our product works." The reader's guard stays down when the message appears to come from a source with no financial interest.

## 4. Social Proof / FOMO

"People are turning to X" beats "You should try X."

Make them feel like they're discovering a movement, not being pitched a product. Trends sell. Ads don't.

**How to deploy it:**

- **Volume:** "47,000 Dog Owners Can't Be Wrong" — numbers create automatic validation
- **Movement:** "Millennials Are Canceling Cable" — show a trend they're late to
- **Skeptic converted:** "She Was Skeptical. 90 Days Later..." — acknowledge their doubt, then overcome it
- **Scarcity:** "Selling Out" — implies limited availability and high demand

**FOMO trigger:** "Should Take Advantage" implies something is on the table you're not grabbing. Others are already getting it. You're being left behind. That's a trigger that overrides rational evaluation.

## 5. Contrarian Framing

Challenge a "known truth" and you own the conversation.

**How it works:**

Everyone over 50 has been told prostate problems come with age. The ad says: wrong. Now the reader is confused about something they thought they understood. Confused people click to resolve the confusion.

Contrarian framing works because it:
- Creates an information gap (what IS the real cause?)
- Makes the reader feel smart for questioning the mainstream
- Promises insider knowledge that contradicts the masses

**Examples:**
- "Has Nothing to Do With Age" — challenges accepted belief
- "What They Don't Tell You" — implies conspiracy or hidden truth
- "The Real Reason Diets Stop Working After 40" — reframes a known frustration

**Warning:** The contrarian claim must feel plausible. Too outrageous and it triggers skepticism instead of curiosity. The sweet spot is a claim that's surprising but not unbelievable.

## 6. Fear + Discovery

Hidden risk + unexpected solution. This combines loss aversion with novelty.

**How it works:**

First, establish a threat the reader didn't know about. "Did Not See This Coming" is a threat wrapped in curiosity. Something changed. Something bad. And you missed it. The amygdala takes over.

Then offer a discovery — something unexpected, simple, and novel. Not a pill. Not a program. Something weird enough to be interesting. Gelatin. A household item. A 15-minute stretch.

**The combination:** Fear gets them to stop scrolling. Discovery gives them a reason to click. Fear alone is depressing. Discovery alone is ignorable. Together, they're unstoppable.

**Key:** The discovery should feel like insider information the mainstream is hiding. "The diet industry doesn't want you to know" framing amplifies this.

## 7. Identity Filtering

Hyper-specific callouts that disqualify 99% of people scrolling — which is exactly the point.

**How it works:**

"Senior Drivers In Idaho" calls one person's name in a crowded room. If you're a senior driver in Idaho, you have to look. Everyone else scrolls. This keeps CPCs low and intent high.

**Layers of specificity:**
- **Age:** "Anyone Over 60" — demographic filter
- **Location:** "In Idaho," "British Homeowners" — geographic tribe
- **Condition:** "If You Have Arthritis" — pain-based filter
- **Role:** "Teachers," "Grandmothers," "Veterans" — identity anchor

**Rule:** The more specific you are, the fewer people you reach — but the ones you reach cannot scroll past. One highly targeted ad beats a broad one every time. Scale by creating multiple specific variants, not by widening the net.

---

## 8. Third-Person Storytelling

Stop selling the product. Sell someone else's result.

**How it works:**

No command. No promise. Just a story about someone else. Stories lower resistance — we lean in instead of defend. When you tell a story about "Sarah, 54, who couldn't walk up stairs," the reader isn't being sold to. They're hearing about a person like them who found something that worked.

**Why it's powerful:** If someone like me did it, why can't I? That's the thought you want. It builds trust and authority without the reader ever feeling pitched.

**Examples:**
- "This new method made Evelyn stop chewing her nails in less than 30 days"
- "'It didn't fit,' Michael (53) shares the secret how women started paying more attention to him again"
- Third-person before/after: "She was skeptical. 90 days later, down 3 dress sizes"

**Key technique — Implied transformation:** Don't state the result explicitly. "Clothes don't fit the same" never says she lost weight. It doesn't give a number. It makes the reader's brain fill in the result. The transformation the reader imagines is always more powerful than the one you state.

**Pair with:** Identity Filtering (make the character match the target demo) and Social Proof (one person's story implies many others).

---

## 9. Tiny Input, Big Output

The gap between a small ask and a big promise is what drives clicks.

**How it works:**

The ad doesn't say she went on the treadmill for 2 hours a day. She just ate gelatin. The smaller the ask, the lower the resistance to try it yourself. "Try tonight," "15 minutes," "one simple trick" — these all signal that the effort is trivial compared to the result.

**Why it's powerful:** Most people have tried hard things that didn't work. They're burned out on effort. When the input is absurdly small and the output is life-changing, the brain says "what do I have to lose?" That's the click.

**Examples:**
- "Do this for 15 minutes to relieve sciatica" — not a lifestyle change, not a 6-week program
- "Eat gelatin" — not a diet, not a workout, just jello
- "Try tonight" — not a 90-day commitment, just tonight
- "One simple trick" — not a protocol, just one thing

**Key rule:** Promise simplicity. One input, one output. The ad that says "do these 5 exercises, change your diet, and take 3 supplements" will always lose to "eat this one food before bed."

**Pair with:** Authority (specialist says do this one thing) and Discovery (unexpected simple solution).

## Positioning Principles

These shape what the ad is actually about — before you write a single word.

### Sell the Life, Not the Product

The product is irrelevant. The transformation is the real product.

A bracelet ad doesn't mention materials, craftsmanship, or price. It shows a man wearing it while an attractive woman looks at him. Men don't buy accessories — they buy how it makes them feel. The bracelet is just the vehicle.

- No feature list. No material specs. No "limited time offer."
- Find the deeper desire behind the purchase: confidence, relief, belonging, status
- "Just a man who looks like he figured something out" — that's the entire ad

**When gathering inputs, always ask: what does the buyer actually want?** Not the product benefit. Not the feature. The life they're trying to get to. Sell that life. The product is just the way to get there.

### Sell the Problem Before the Solution

The ad's first job is to make the reader *feel* the pain — not to hint at an answer.

"We all know someone who forgot. We're all scared it could be us." That's a dementia supplement ad. It doesn't mention the supplement. It doesn't mention a fix. It finds the wound and presses on it. The cure comes later, in the advertorial.

- Lead with the problem, not the product
- Activate fear, frustration, or insecurity before offering any resolution
- People with nerve pain have been let down a hundred times — they don't believe big promises anymore. But they'll click on something that perfectly describes their suffering

**Sequence:** Problem activation → curiosity about solution → click → advertorial explains → product page sells. The ad only owns step one.

## Copywriting Principles

These aren't psychological mechanisms — they're execution rules for how to write the actual words.

### Use Relatable Symptoms, Not Diagnoses

"Pants keep tightening" — nobody googles that. But everyone feels it. Use the language of daily experience, not clinical terms.

- "Achy shoulders" beats "rotator cuff inflammation"
- "Can't sleep through the night" beats "insomnia"
- "Energy crashes at 2pm" beats "chronic fatigue"

The reader should think "that's me" — not "I should ask my doctor about that."

### Scarcity Through "Only"

"The *only* olive oil a cardiologist recommends" does different work than "the *best* olive oil." "Only" implies everything else is worse. It eliminates the need for research. No comparison shopping. Just this one.

### The Visual That Demands Answers

Sometimes the image IS the curiosity gap. A hand holding nuts next to a diabetes headline. Pomegranate? Beets? Something else? The brain needs the dopamine of finding out. When the image itself raises a question, you've doubled your open loops — one from the headline, one from the image.

## Stacking Principles

The strongest ads combine 2-3 of these mechanisms:

- **Curiosity Gap + Enemy Framing:** "The common food that's silently destroying your joints" (loop open + trusted thing is a threat)
- **Authority + Curiosity Gap:** "Rheumatologist Says Please Stop Eating This Food If You Have Arthritis" (expert credibility + unnamed food)
- **Identity Filtering + Fear + Discovery:** "Senior Drivers In Idaho Did Not See This Coming" (specific audience + threat + unknown information)
- **Social Proof + Curiosity Gap:** "People Are Turning to Gelatin for Stubborn Belly Fat" (trend + bizarre connection)
- **Contrarian + Authority:** "Plastic Surgeon Reveals Why Collagen Is Making Wrinkles Worse" (expert challenges trusted product)

When combining, make sure each element has a clear job. Don't overload. The best native ads do 2-3 things with surgical precision.
