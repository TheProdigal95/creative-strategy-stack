# Eugene Schwartz Copywriting Framework

A comprehensive reference for writing, critiquing, and analyzing copy using Eugene Schwartz's principles from *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966) and his collected teachings from speeches and seminars.

**Track record:** 200+ winning direct-response ads with an 85% hit ratio. Over $200 million in book sales for Rodale Press. Wrote the founding ad that grew Boardroom Inc. from $3,500 to over $50 million in annual revenue.

---

## Part I: The Philosophical Foundation

### The Governing Law

> "Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears and desires that already exist in the hearts of millions of people, and focus those already-existing desires onto a particular product."

This is not a suggestion — it is the governing law of the entire framework. Every technique, every headline formula, every structural decision flows from this truth. Your job is not to convince. It's to **channel existing desire** onto your product.

### What Creates Mass Desire

**Mass desire** is "the public spread of a private want." Two categories of forces create it:

**Permanent forces:**
- Mass instinct — primal desires for attractiveness, health, virility
- Mass problems — ongoing frustrations people repeatedly try to solve

**Forces of change:**
- Trends — rising and falling cultural currents
- Mass education — evolving knowledge that reshapes expectations

### The Three Dimensions of Desire

Every mass desire has three measurable dimensions that determine which desire to lead with:

| Dimension | Question | Example |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Urgency/Intensity** | How desperately do they want this resolved? | Chronic arthritic pain > minor headache |
| **Staying Power** | How frequently is this desire felt? Can it ever be permanently satisfied? | Raw hunger recurs; gourmet craving is intermittent |
| **Scope** | How many people share this desire? | Millions > thousands |

> "Your choice among these mass desires is the most important step of the entire copywriting process."

### The Three-Step Process

1. **Choose** the most powerful desire your product can satisfy
2. **Acknowledge** that desire in your headline
3. **Show** how your product's performances satisfy that desire

Instead of asking: "What does my product do?"

Ask: **"What transformation does my audience already crave?"**

## Part II: The Five Levels of Market Awareness

The awareness framework answers: **"How much does your prospect already know about the way your product satisfies their desire?"** It determines where your copy must begin, what information to include, and how long the copy needs to be.

> "The more aware your market, the easier the selling job, the less you need to say."

### Level 1: Most Aware (~3% of market)

The prospect knows your product by name, knows what it does, and wants it. They simply haven't purchased yet.

**Copy approach:** Name the product prominently plus a deal. That's it. No education needed. Price, discount, urgency, and a clear CTA.

**Example:** *"[Product Name] — Now 50% off. Today only."*

**Biggest mistake:** Over-explaining. These people need a reason to act now, not more information.

**Modern application:** Retargeting ads, abandoned cart emails, flash sales to subscribers, loyalty offers.

### Level 2: Product Aware

The prospect knows your product exists but isn't fully convinced. They may not know all it does or may doubt how well it works.

**Copy approach:** Display product name prominently. Use the rest to prove superiority.

**Seven headline tasks for this level:**
1. Reinforce desire for your product
2. Sharpen the image of how it satisfies desire
3. Extend the image to new contexts
4. Introduce new proof or documentation
5. Announce a new mechanism
6. Announce a mechanism that eliminates former limitations
7. Completely change the image to remove it from competition

**Modern application:** Product comparison pages, feature highlights, testimonial-driven ads, demo videos, "what's new" announcements.

### Level 3: Solution Aware

The prospect knows they want what your product does — they recognize the solution category — but they don't know your specific product exists.

**Copy approach:** Do NOT lead with your product name; it means nothing to them. Lead with the desire or the solution.

**Three-step headline process:**
1. Name the desire and/or its solution
2. Prove the solution can be accomplished
3. Show that the mechanism of accomplishment is contained in your product

**Modern application:** SEO content targeting solution-category keywords ("best project management tools"), social ads leading with the outcome, comparison content.

### Level 4: Problem Aware

The prospect feels a pain, frustration, or unfulfilled need but doesn't know solutions exist. They know something is wrong but haven't articulated it.

**Copy approach:** Name the need in the headline. Dramatize it so vividly the prospect realizes how badly they need a solution. Then present your product as the inevitable answer.

Do NOT mention your product name or price in the headline — both are irrelevant at this stage. Find the **"point of maximum anxiety"** — the emotional peak of their frustration — and connect there first.

**Modern application:** Content addressing pain points, social ads that open with empathy ("Does this sound familiar?"), quiz funnels, educational sequences.

### Level 5: Completely Unaware (~60% of market)

The prospect doesn't know they have a problem, won't admit it, or the need is so vague it resists being summarized. This is the largest segment and the hardest to reach.

**Copy approach:** Schwartz's elimination process strips away everything: no price (meaningless), no product name (meaningless), no direct statement of what the product does (won't work).

What's left? **Your market.** Write an **identification headline** — selling nothing, promising nothing, but echoing an emotion, attitude, or satisfaction that picks people out of the crowd and defines them.

**Sub-strategies:**
- Give words to a hidden dream
- Exploit a hidden fear
- Project an ultimate triumph
- Use a common resentment
- Lead into an unacceptable problem through a universally accepted image

**Modern application:** Brand awareness campaigns, storytelling video content, viral/shareable content, influencer content.

### How to Identify Awareness Level

Ask these diagnostic questions in sequence:

| Signal | Awareness Level |
|--------|-----------------|
| Searching for your brand name? | Most Aware |
| Comparing you to competitors? | Product Aware |
| Searching for "best [solution type]"? | Solution Aware |
| Searching for "why does [problem] happen"? | Problem Aware |
| Not searching at all? | Unaware |

**Additional signals:** Customer survey responses, Facebook audience composition (cold interest-based = Unaware/Problem Aware; retargeting = Product/Most Aware), landing page analytics, social listening for how people describe problems.

### Copy Length by Awareness

| Level | Length | What's Needed |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| Most Aware | Shortest | Name + deal |
| Product Aware | Medium | Proof and differentiation |
| Solution Aware | Medium-Long | Mechanism and benefits |
| Problem Aware | Long | Education + agitation + solution |
| Unaware | Longest | Story-driven, indirect entry through every stage |

## Part III: The Five Stages of Market Sophistication

While awareness measures the individual prospect's knowledge, **sophistication measures the market's collective exposure** to competing claims.

Diagnostic question: "How many similar products have been presented to them before yours?"

**Awareness tells you WHAT to say. Sophistication tells you HOW DIFFERENT you need to be.**

### Stage 1: First to Market

You are the first (or among the first) to offer this type of product. No competition, no precedent.

**Strategy:** Be simple and direct. The innovation IS the message. State the claim plainly. Don't be fancy. Dramatize the claim in body copy and prove it works.

**Example:** *"I Am 61 Pounds Lighter… Never A Hungry Minute."*

**Identification signal:** No competitors making similar claims. Audience responds enthusiastically to simple promises.

### Stage 2: Competition Arrives

Competitors have entered. The direct claim still works, but you must push it to the absolute limit.

**Strategy:** Copy the successful claim from Stage 1 but enlarge it with bigger promises, more specifics, more superlatives. Outbid competition.

**Example:** Expanding from garden results claims to *"Six Hundred Mums From A Single Bush!"*

**Identification signal:** A few competitors making similar claims. Market still responds to direct claims but needs more conviction.

### Stage 3: The Market is Skeptical

The market has heard all the claims in all their extremes. Simple repetition or exaggeration won't work.

**Strategy:** Introduce a **mechanism** — the unique method, system, process, ingredient, or technology that delivers the promised benefit.

> "What this market needs now is a new device to make all these old claims become fresh and believable again — A NEW MECHANISM."

The emphasis shifts from WHAT the product does to HOW it works. The mechanism answers: "I've heard this promise before — why should I believe it now?"

**Example:** Weight loss copy shifts from "lose weight fast" to *"Floats Fat Right Out of Your Body!"*

**Identification signal:** Competitors making similar promises. Audience is jaded and skeptical. They've tried similar products and been disappointed.

### Stage 4: Mechanism Sophistication

Competitors have introduced mechanisms too. The market has heard mechanism claims before.

**Strategy:** Apply the same enlargement strategy from Stage 2, but to the mechanism: make it easier, quicker, surer, able to solve more of the problem, free from old limitations. Show why YOUR mechanism is superior.

**Example:** *"FIRST NO-DIET REDUCING WONDER DRUG!"*

**Identification signal:** Multiple competitors promote mechanisms. Audience compares HOW products work. You need to demonstrate process superiority.

### Stage 5: Complete Skepticism

The market doesn't believe advertising. Claims are irrelevant. Mechanisms seem gimmicky.

**Strategy:** The emphasis shifts from promise and mechanism to **identification with the prospect himself**. Bring the prospect into your ad not through desire but through identity — echo their dominant thoughts, name their feelings, define who they are.

> "The emphasis shifts from the promise and the mechanism which accomplishes it, to identification with the prospect himself."

**Two types of identity roles:**
- **Character roles** — progressive, liberal, sophisticated (traits that already belong to the customer)
- **Achievement roles** — executive, entrepreneur, homeowner (status positions earned)

At this stage, brands win through emotional connection, not features. Nike sells identity, not shoe specifications. Apple sells to "dreamers challenging the status quo," not computing power.

**Identification signal:** Market is completely saturated. Audience actively looks for reasons NOT to buy. Nobody believes direct advertising.

### How Sophistication and Awareness Interact

These are two independent dimensions forming a grid:

- **Problem Aware + Stage 1** = Simple problem-to-solution copy
- **Product Aware + Stage 5** = Identity-based messaging
- **Low awareness + Low sophistication** = Educate broadly and simply
- **High awareness + High sophistication** = Differentiate through identity and mechanism innovation

Most modern DTC categories (skincare, supplements, fitness) operate at **Stage 3–5**, making mechanism and identification strategies essential.

## Part IV: The Mechanism

A mechanism is **the unique manner, method, material, or process that allows a product to deliver its promised benefits**. It is distinct from a USP: while a USP states your competitive advantage (the RESULT), a mechanism promotes the PROCESS — the HOW behind the what.

### How to Find or Create a Mechanism

1. **Study your product deeply** for any proprietary process, ingredient, technology, or method
2. **Name it** — give it a proprietary name (e.g., "GL-70" for Gleem toothpaste)
3. **Describe it** — explain how it works in vivid, specific terms
4. **Feature it** — make it the centerpiece of your headline and copy

### Three Stages of Mechanism Use

1. Simply **name** the mechanism
2. **Describe** how it works
3. **Feature** it as the star of the entire ad

### Positioning the Mechanism

Two options within an ad:
- **Inside body copy** — to prove the main claim
- **On top of the ad** — as the main claim itself

At Stage 3 sophistication, the mechanism becomes the headline. At Stage 4, you elaborate the mechanism to show why yours is superior.

**Critical rule:**

> "Never turn the copy into scientific discourse. You must load it with promise, load it with emotion."

### Mechanism Examples

| Weak (WHAT) | Strong (HOW) |
|-------------|--------------|
| "Lose 10 lbs in 2 weeks" | "Flushes fat directly from your cells in 2 weeks" |
| "Vitamin supplement" | "Proprietary absorption technology delivers nutrients directly to cells" |
| "Effective skincare" | "Patented triple-extraction process concentrates 3x more active ingredients" |

## Part V: Headlines — The 38 Verbalization Techniques

> "Your headline has only one job — to stop your prospect and compel him to read the second sentence of your ad."

The headline does not sell. It does not need to mention the product. It does not even need to mention the main appeal. Its only purpose is to stop the hand from throwing the piece away.

### Before Writing Any Headline

Answer three questions:
1. What mass desire motivates this market?
2. What is the prospect's state of awareness?
3. What is the market's stage of sophistication?

These three answers determine your headline's approach. Once you have the right approach, use these 38 techniques to **strengthen the expression**.

### The Complete 38 Techniques

**Measurement & Size:**
1. **Measure the SIZE** — "20,000 Filter Traps in Viceroy!"
2. **Measure the SPEED** — "In Two Seconds, Bayer Aspirin Begins to Dissolve in Your Glass!"
3. **COMPARE the claim** — "Six Times Whiter Washes!"

**Sensory & Dramatic:**
4. **METAPHORIZE** — "Melts Away Ugly Fat!"
5. **SENSITIZE** (make them feel/smell/touch/see/hear it) — "Tastes Like You Just Picked It!"
6. **DEMONSTRATE with a prime example** — "At 60 Miles an Hour, the Loudest Noise in This Rolls Royce Is the Electric Clock!"
7. **DRAMATIZE the claim or result** — "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano — But When I Started to Play…"

**Paradox & Surprise:**
8. **State as PARADOX** — "How a Bald-Headed Barber Saved My Hair!"
9. **REMOVE LIMITATIONS** — "Shrinks Hemorrhoids Without Surgery!"
10. **SURPRISE** — "See What Happens When You Crush a Hartman DC-8? Nothing!"

**Authority & Association:**
11. **ASSOCIATE with values/people** prospect admires — "9 Out of 10 Decorators Use Wunda-Weave Carpets"
12. **Tie AUTHORITY into claim** — "Here's What Doctors Do When They Feel Rotten!"
13. **Bring in an AUDIENCE of peers** — testimonials, case studies

**Detail & Proof:**
14. **Show HOW MUCH WORK in detail** — "Now! Relief from All 5 Acid-Caused Stomach Troubles — in Seconds!"
15. **BEFORE-AND-AFTER** — Contrasting the state before vs. after the product
16. **Dramatize HOW HARD to produce** — "When Jens Finished Designing This Candleholder We Had to Invent a Whole New Kind of Candle."

**Newness & Exclusivity:**
17. **Stress NEWNESS** — "Announcing! Guided Missile Spark Plugs!"
18. **Stress EXCLUSIVITY** — "Only Gleem Has GL-70"

**Questions & Challenges:**
19. **State as QUESTION** — "Who Else Wants a Whiter Wash — with No Hard Work?"
20. **Turn into CHALLENGE** — "Which Twin Has the Toni?"
21. **Turn into QUESTION AND ANSWER** — "You Don't Know What's Under the Hood… Who Should You See? Someone Who Cares — United Delco."
22. **CHALLENGE limiting beliefs** — "You Are Twice as Smart as You Think."

**Information & Warning:**
23. **Offer INFORMATION** — "How to Win Friends and Influence People!"
24. **Offer INFORMATION IN THE AD ITSELF** — "Why Men Crack…"
25. **WARN about pitfalls** — "Don't Invest One Cent Until You Check This Guide!"

**Case History & Quotation:**
26. **State as CASE-HISTORY QUOTATION** — "Look Mom — No Cavities!"
27. **Turn into CASE HISTORY** — "Again She Orders — 'A Chicken Salad, Please'"

**Mechanism & Connection:**
28. **CONNECT MECHANISM to claim** — "Floats Fat Right Out of Your Body!"
29. **STARTLE by contradicting expected mechanism** — "'Hit Hell Out of the Ball with Your Right Hand,' Says Tommy Armour!"
30. **CONNECT NEED AND CLAIM** — "There Is Only One Solution to an Advertising Problem: Find the Man!"
31. **Show the DIFFERENCE** — "The Difference in Premium Gasolines Is Right in the Additives!"

**Problem Naming:**
32. **GIVE A NAME to the problem** — "When You're Weary with Day-Time Fatigue, Take Alka-Seltzer"

**Emphasis & Language:**
33. **EMPHASIZE by phraseology** — "Nobody but Nobody Undersells Gimbel's!"
34. **CONDENSE/INTERCHANGE** — "Pour Yourself a New Engine!"
35. **SYMBOLIZE with parallel** — "Starting Next Tuesday, the Atlantic Ocean Becomes Only One-Fifth as Long!"

**Ease & Accessibility:**
36. **Show how EASY** — "If You Can Count to Eleven, You Can Increase Your Speed at Numbers!"

**Targeting:**
37. **Address people who CAN'T buy** — "If You've Already Taken Your Vacation, Don't Read This."
38. **ADDRESS the prospect's role** — "For the Man Who Will Settle for Nothing Less Than the Presidency of His Firm."

**Bonus: Accusation:**
- **Accuse of being TOO GOOD** — "Is It Immoral to Make Money This Easily?"

### The Four Elements of Powerful Headlines

The best headlines combine:
1. **Promise** — the main benefit/intrigue
2. **Mechanism** — the new method that delivers it
3. **Proof** — evidence it's been delivered to people like you
4. **Deep emotion** — visceral gut connection

All four combined compel the prospect to read.

### Headlines by Awareness Level

**Unaware** — Lead with emotion, curiosity, or story:
- "The Strange Secret of the Woman Who Never Ages"
- "Why Some People Almost Always Make Money in the Stock Market"

**Problem Aware** — Dramatize the problem:
- "Tired of Diets That Don't Work?"
- "When Day-Time Fatigue Makes Everything Harder..."

**Solution Aware** — Introduce your mechanism:
- "The New Science of Fat-Flushing"
- "How a Simple 5-Minute Routine Builds Muscle Faster"

**Product Aware** — Differentiate and prove:
- "Why [Product] Outperforms [Competitor] 3-to-1"
- "See What 10,000 Customers Already Know About [Product]"

**Most Aware** — Just make the offer:
- "[Product Name] — Now 40% Off"
- "Your [Product] is Waiting — Free Shipping Today"

## Part VI: The Seven Techniques of Breakthrough Copy

These are the core structural tools for building the body of any advertisement.

### 1. Intensification

**"The presenting of a series of fresh, new, and different fulfillments for your prospect's dominant desire."**

Like burning paper with a magnifying glass — you don't create sunlight, you focus it until it ignites.

**The 13 Intensification Sub-Techniques:**

1. **Present the product directly** — blunt, detailed description of what it does
2. **Put claims in action** — show HOW it delivers benefits
3. **Bring in the reader** — tell them what happens the first day they own it
4. **Show how to test claims** — let them visualize proving the performance
5. **Stretch benefits in time** — show results over weeks and months
6. **Bring in an audience** — testimonials providing fresh perspectives
7. **Show experts approving** — authority proof
8. **Compare, contrast, prove superiority** — side-by-side with alternatives
9. **Picture the black side** — agitate the problem ("irritate the wound, then apply the salve")
10. **Show how easy** — reduce perceived effort while contrasting with tremendous benefits
11. **Use metaphor, analogy, imagination** — present facts beyond rigid realism
12. **Summarize** — the "catalog" (condensed listing of all performances) and piling desire upon desire
13. **Put your guarantee to work** — turn the guarantee into a climactic reassertion

### 2. Identification

While intensification amplifies desire for what a product DOES, identification works on **WHO the product makes you BECOME**.

> "At least half of all purchases made today cannot be understood in terms of function alone."

Every product should offer two reasons for buying:
- Fulfillment of a physical want
- A method of fulfillment that **defines the buyer to the outside world**

**Two types of identity roles:**
- **Character roles** — progressive, sophisticated, creative (traits the customer already claims)
- **Achievement roles** — executive, entrepreneur, homeowner (status positions earned)

At Stage 5 sophistication, identification becomes the primary selling tool.

### 3. Gradualization

**"The art of starting your ad with a statement that will be immediately and entirely accepted, and then building a subsequent chain of acceptances."**

The purpose is to lead the reader to a conclusion they would not have accepted without the preliminary statements.

> "Never violate your prospect's beliefs."

Beliefs cannot be changed, only channeled. Build a **"bridge of belief"** — if you demand the prospect jump across a believability chasm, the ad fails. But if you build a bridge starting on their side, they'll let you lead them almost anywhere.

> "One fully-believed promise has ten times the sales power of ten partially-believed promises."

**Believability devices:**
- The inclusion question (ask something they'd answer "yes" to)
- Detailed identification ("Did you buy your TV after 1974?")
- The language of logic ("simply because," "therefore," "here's why")
- Syllogistic thinking ("the bigger the spark, the bigger the explosion — the bigger the explosion, the faster the car goes")
- Promise-belief interlacing (every promise immediately followed by proof)

### 4. Redefinition

Presenting the product in a new light to differentiate from competitors and remove roadblocks before they arise. Preemptive objection handling through reframing.

### 5. Mechanization

Explaining HOW the product works to give prospects a tangible reason to believe. Deploying the unique mechanism within body copy — naming it, describing it, and featuring it.

### 6. Concentration

Focusing the entire message on a single compelling idea.

Can also be used offensively: **"the careful, logical, documented process of proving ineffectual other ways of satisfying your prospect's desire."**

Critical caveat: if you can only attack competitors without simultaneously showing how your product provides what theirs lacks, say nothing.

### 7. Camouflage

Presenting the advertisement as informative content rather than selling. Advertorials, native ads, and editorial-style content.

> "The more your advertisement looks like a news article, the more readers it will attract."

## Part VII: Copy Structure

### "Copy is not written. Copy is assembled."

> "You do not write copy, you assemble it. You are working with a series of building blocks, you are putting the building blocks together, and then are putting them in certain structures. You are building a little city of desire for your person to come and live in."

### The Chain of Reading

Every element of copy has one job: get the prospect to read the next element.

- The headline's job → get them to read the first sentence
- The first sentence → must compel the second sentence
- This creates a **chain of reading** where no link can break

### Promise-Belief Interlacing

Build belief simultaneously with desire:

> "Your objective is to build belief at the same exact time that you build desire."

Interlace each new promise with proof — use language signals that show it logically follows from everything already proven.

**Patterns:**
- Promise-belief-promise variation (every promise immediately followed by proof)
- Paragraph parallelism (the same word structure from an accepted statement is picked up and used to borrow acceptance for a fresh claim)

### Emotional Escalation

Model copy like action movies: the opening grabs you, then it builds plot, then another explosion.

**"Face smashing against the windshield"** copy — keeping readers hooked with continuous momentum.

**Two types of momentum:**
- **Structural momentum** — the logical flow of claims building on each other
- **Transition momentum** — using "momentum-phrases" (transitional devices that prevent interest from flagging)

**Core rule for every sentence:**

> "No sentence can be effective if it contains facts alone. It must also contain emotion, image, logic, and promise."

### The Close

- Call out the reader with "you"
- Make them imagine having already decided ("are yours")
- Reframe the decision from buy/don't buy to current-life versus better-life
- Write coupons in first-person "I" form to force conscious commitment
- Always include money-back guarantees emphasized as zero-risk

## Part VIII: The Three Dimensions of Your Prospect

Every prospect has:

1. **Desires** — What they want
2. **Identifications** — Who they want to be
3. **Beliefs** — What they accept as true

### Using Identification

> "Each of us must know where we belong. When we don't, we become tremendously uncomfortable."

- Show how your product fits the role they aspire to
- Connect to identity groups they want to join
- Make them feel the product is "for people like them"

### Respecting Beliefs

> "One fully-believed promise has ten times the sales power of ten partially-believed promises."

- Never violate established beliefs
- Build on what they already accept
- Use their language, not yours

## Part IX: Schwartz's Working Methods

### The 33:33 Method

Schwartz worked **3 hours per day, 5 days per week**, broken into sessions of precisely **33 minutes and 33 seconds**.

**The rules:**
1. Set timer for 33:33
2. Sit at desk — you can drink coffee, stare out the window, sit doing nothing, or work
3. You CANNOT leave your chair
4. You CANNOT do anything unrelated
5. When the timer rings, stop immediately (even mid-sentence)
6. Take a 10–15 minute break
7. Repeat

**The Montana metaphor:**

> "When a posse used to chase a criminal, the horse thief would ride for an hour, then get off and walk the horse for an hour. Why? Because the horse would be exhausted and drop dead. Your mind has a way of dropping dead on you."

During breaks, his subconscious continued working. His biggest breakthroughs came while shaving, showering, or making coffee.

**Results:** No writer's block. Ever. 85% hit rate on ads.

### The 5-Week Research-to-Copy Framework

For a typical project with a 1,000+ page manuscript:

**Weeks 1–2: Deep research**
- Read every single word
- Deliberately skip the table of contents to encounter each claim fresh
- Underline every statement that makes a significant claim
- Goal: absorb the guts, heart, and meat

**Week 3: Organization**
- Type all important claims into one master document (~50–60 pages)
- Separate which statements moved you most
- This becomes an **"associative stimulus list"**
- Add your own ideas in bold/italics to distinguish from original quotes

**Week 4: Assembly**
- Begin assembling copy
- Leave title and subtitles for LAST
- Alternate between writing different pieces (non-linear process)
- Search for illustrations

**Week 5: Polishing**
- Re-read the manuscript and notes one final time
- Make adjustments
- Edit and re-edit

> "Research is 80–90% of the copywriting work."

### Staying Connected to the Market

> "If you don't spend at least two hours a week finding out where your market is today, you are finished! You will have a career of three blazing years and be finished."

Schwartz read the National Enquirer every week, saw every film that earned over $100 million, talked to cab drivers, read People Magazine and Weekly World News — anything to stay connected to ordinary people's language, desires, and capacity for belief.

## Part X: The Lesser-Known Teachings

### From the Phillips Publishing Seminar (October 8, 1993)

**Schwartz's 8 Rules of Marketing:**

1. **Be the best listener you ever met** — Talk little, listen much. Read what your market reads.
2. **Work extremely intensely, in spurts** — The 33:33 timer method.
3. **Never "create"** — Know the product to its core and combine details in new ways.
4. **Write to the chimpanzee brain** — Simply and directly. Don't overestimate intelligence.
5. **Channel demand, never sell** — Take existing desire and direct it to your product.
6. **Think about what your product DOES, not IS** — Focus on performances, not descriptions.
7. **Make gratification instantaneous** — Within the copy itself, the prospect should already begin to feel they're getting something.
8. **Fail often, test big differences** — Shows you're trying hard enough.

**The Boardroom Origin Story:**

Marty Edelston came to Schwartz with $3,500 total capital. Schwartz charged $2,500 as a copy fee. They talked for 4 hours. About 30 minutes in, Edelston said one sentence that became the headline:

*"How To Get the Heart of 370 Business Magazines in Just 30 Minutes a Month."*

Schwartz's copy was **70% Edelston's own conversation**. He wrote the entire ad that same night while his wife was putting on makeup — but waited two weeks to deliver it "because he would think it was worth nothing."

That ad launched a $50 million+ company.

**On creativity as connectivity:**

> "Creation means make something out of nothing. So let's throw creation out and talk about connectivity. What you are trying to do is connect things together. You're trying to get two ideas that were separate in your mind before and put them together so they are now one thought."

### From the Rodale Press Workshop (May 11, 1994)

**On competition through preparation:**

> "I will take the best copywriter in this room, who is sloppy and careless, and match him against a good copy cub. And 2 out of 3 times, the sloppiness of the great person will be beaten by the carefulness of the other person."

**On ugly layouts selling:**

Schwartz advocated deliberately crude layouts that could increase sales by 20–30%. His famous "Burn Disease Out of Your Body" ad cost $125 for layout — small type, crude design. It ran for **14+ years**, selling a book that went from $6.95 to $33, with sales increasing each year.

**On the "creative feeding frenzy":**

> "You want to go into a creative frenzy. You want to have the ideas come so much into your mind that you can't stop."

He worked on multiple unrelated projects simultaneously — while writing a biography about Jesus Christ in 33 AD, he would get "marvelous ideas for Rodale."

**On finding hidden desires:**

> "They are hidden, because your reader doesn't want to really talk about them, but they are in the subculture, hidden culture, under-culture of our civilization."

## Part XI: Evaluating Copy Quality

### The Research Test

> "I can see where they didn't do enough work. I can see where they lost facts. The loss of those facts stands out so clearly because I had the facts; they didn't."

### The "You" Test

> "What you are talking about is YOU. The person who has got this piece of paper in their hand. You, you, you, you, you. If it doesn't have the word 'you' a hundred times, I really don't like it very much."

### The Transparency Test

Good copy is invisible — like a clean window at a toy store. The reader should see the "toys" (benefits) inside, not notice the glass.

> "If they compliment your writing style, sophisticated language, impressive words, cleverness — you know you've got a real stinker."

### The Chimpanzee Brain Test

Write simply and directly. Copy must be understood by anyone. Jargon kills conversions.

### The Three-Dimension Test

Every ad must work on three fronts simultaneously:
1. How much copy is needed to build desire?
2. How much to get the prospect to imagine life with the product?
3. How much to counter objections and build belief?

The answers determine length, structure, and pace.

### The Chain-of-Acceptances Test

Is the copy structured as a chain where each statement builds logically on the last?

> "We are creating a stream of acceptances. This is the essence of building your ad."

### Common Failure Causes

- Trying to create desire instead of channeling it
- Violating prospect beliefs
- Demanding a believability chasm jump
- Being too clever or literary
- Insufficient research
- Writing to the wrong awareness level or sophistication stage
- Repetition without variation
- Selling features instead of performances
- Leading with the product to an unaware audience

## Part XII: Copy Critique Checklist

### Market Alignment
- [ ] Does the copy channel an existing mass desire (not try to create one)?
- [ ] Is the single dominant desire clearly identified and featured?
- [ ] Is the headline/hook matched to the prospect's actual awareness level?
- [ ] Does the sophistication approach match the market's current stage?

### Headline/Hook Quality
- [ ] Does it stop the prospect and compel further reading/watching?
- [ ] Does it combine promise + mechanism + proof + emotion?
- [ ] Does it call out the target audience for self-selection?
- [ ] Is it believable at the market's current sophistication level?

### Body Copy Structure
- [ ] Does every sentence reward the reader's attention?
- [ ] Is intensification present (desire amplifying throughout)?
- [ ] Is identification used (reader sees themselves)?
- [ ] Is there a clear mechanism explaining HOW the product works?
- [ ] Is the argument built gradually through a chain of acceptances?
- [ ] Are objections addressed before they arise?
- [ ] Is the copy focused on one main idea?

### Close Quality
- [ ] Is there clear urgency?
- [ ] Is risk removed (guarantee, trial)?
- [ ] Is the CTA appropriate for the awareness level?
- [ ] Does the close reframe the decision as current-life versus better-life?

## Part XIII: Practical Application for DTC and Meta Advertising

### Mapping Awareness to Facebook Ad Funnels

| Schwartz Level | Funnel Stage | Traffic Type | Meta Targeting | Creative Approach |
|----------------|--------------|--------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Unaware** | Top of Funnel | Cold | Interest/Lookalike | Educational/entertaining video; problem identification; curiosity hooks; NO product mention |
| **Problem Aware** | Top of Funnel | Cold-Warm | Interest + Engagement retargeting | Problem-centric content; validate concerns; empathy-first hooks |
| **Solution Aware** | Mid-Funnel | Warm | Video viewer retargeting; website visitors | Comparison content; solution education; introduce product; social proof |
| **Product Aware** | Mid-Funnel | Warm-Hot | Page engagers; add-to-cart audiences | Product demos; testimonials; competitive comparison; address objections |
| **Most Aware** | Bottom of Funnel | Hot | Past purchasers; cart abandoners | Direct offers; discounts; urgency/scarcity; simplified CTAs |

### Sophistication Strategies for Crowded DTC Categories

Most DTC markets (skincare, supplements, fitness, fashion) operate at Stage 3–5.

**Stage 3 (most supplement/skincare brands):**
Lead with a unique mechanism. Instead of "lose weight fast," explain HOW: "Our proprietary compound signals your body to convert stored fat into energy."

**Stage 4 (competitive categories):**
Amplify the mechanism: "Our patented triple-extraction process delivers 3x more bioavailable nutrients than standard supplements."

**Stage 5 (saturated markets like fashion):**
Connect with identity. Instead of promoting protein powder benefits, show the lifestyle and community. Glossier succeeds through identity-driven beauty. Outdoor Voices through identity-driven fitness.

### Mechanism-Based Copy Structure for Meta Ads

For Stage 3–4 markets:

1. **Hook:** "Here's why your vitamin D supplement isn't working…" (problem with old mechanism)
2. **Agitation:** "Most brands use D2, which your body can barely absorb…" (expose the failure)
3. **New mechanism:** "Our formula uses bioavailable D3 combined with K2 for 3x absorption…" (introduce your mechanism)
4. **Proof:** "That's why 50,000 customers switched in the last 6 months." (social proof)
5. **CTA:** Matched to awareness level

### Generating New Messaging Angles Systematically

**Step 1 — Desire mining:**
List all mass desires your product touches (health, status, belonging, convenience, pleasure). Rate each by intensity, staying power, and scope. Pick the top 3.

**Step 2 — Mechanism discovery:**
List every unique aspect of HOW your product works. Identify proprietary processes, ingredients, technologies. Name each mechanism.

**Step 3 — Angle testing matrix:**
Create a grid with desires as rows and mechanisms as columns. Each cell = a unique ad angle to test. 3 desires × 3 mechanisms = 9 distinct angles.

**Step 4 — Match to awareness:**
For each winning angle, create variations matched to each awareness level:
- Identification hook (Unaware)
- Problem-agitation hook (Problem Aware)
- Solution-comparison hook (Solution Aware)
- Superiority proof (Product Aware)
- Direct offer (Most Aware)

## Part XIV: Key Quotes — The Essential Schwartz Canon

### On Desire and Markets

> "The power, the force, the overwhelming urge to own that makes advertising work, comes from the market itself, and not from the copy."

> "Mass desire is the public spread of a private want."

> "The greatest mistake marketers make is trying to create demand."

> "Every product gives you dozens of keys. But only one will fit the lock."

### On Copy and Writing

> "Copy is not written. Copy is assembled. You are building a little city of desire for your person to come and live in."

> "No sentence can be effective if it contains facts alone. It must also contain emotion, image, logic, and promise."

> "Your headline has only one job — to stop your prospect and compel him to read the second sentence of your ad."

> "One fully-believed promise has ten times the sales power of ten partially-believed promises."

### On Creativity

> "The creativity is not in you. Never mistake that. The creativity is in your market and in your product, and all you are doing is joining the two together."

> "What you are doing when you are being creative is trying to connect two separate ideas that logically would not go together up until that moment."

### On Preparation

> "I'm better, result-wise, than many great copywriters, who are better writers than I am, because I work harder than they do."

> "A very good copywriter is going to fail. If the guy doesn't fail, he's no good. He's got to fail."

> "If you don't spend at least two hours a week finding out where your market is today, you are finished!"

### On the Prospect

> "If you demand that your prospect jumps across a believability chasm, your ad will fail. If, however, you build a bridge of ideas starting on his side, he will let you lead him almost anywhere."

> "Breakthrough Advertising is not about building better mousetraps. It is about building larger mice — and then building a terrifying fear of them in your customers."

> "Your prospect will give you his attention only as long as you continue to reward it."
