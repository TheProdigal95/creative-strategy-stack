# Point Structure

## General Rules

Each point starts with an image placeholder, then a bold headline, then body copy.

**Format:**

```
[ IMG HERE ]

**#[X]: [Point Headline]**

[Body copy: 2-5 sentences, broken into individual lines]
```

**Per-point word count:** 30-70 words. Mechanism and proof points may need the upper range. If a point needs more than 70 words, it is doing too much.

## Point Headline Criteria

Every point headline must do three things:

1. **Name the feature or benefit** — something concrete the product has or the user gets. Not a vague claim.
2. **Connect to the theme** — the headline should feel at home under the main headline. It layers onto the theme, not away from it.
3. **Position against competition** (where applicable) — for competitive arguments, name the specific alternative that loses at this feature. For other argument types, the contrast can be implicit.

A reader who skims only the headlines, no body copy, should get the full picture: what the product does, why it serves their specific situation, and why alternatives fall short.

### Headline Examples by Argument Type

**Mechanism (theme: busy dads who want to look how they used to):**
- Weak: "Ergonomic Handles Lock Your Form"
- Strong: "Ergonomic Handles Mean You Don't Need A Personal Trainer To Get Your Form Right"
- Why: The strong version names the feature (ergonomic handles), connects to the theme (no time/money for a trainer), and implies the persona's constraint.

**Competitive (theme: busy dads who want to look how they used to):**
- Weak: "Better Than Other Core Exercises"
- Strong: "3 Minutes Beats An Hour At The Gym, And You Don't Even Need To Leave The House"
- Why: Names the feature (3 minutes), connects to the theme (looking how you used to without the time commitment), positions against a specific competitor (the gym, with its hour-plus-commute requirement).

**Social Proof (theme: busy dads who want to look how they used to):**
- Weak: "4.8 Stars From 2,400 Reviews"
- Strong: "2,400 Reviews And Dads Keep Saying The Same Thing: 'I Can See My Abs Again'"
- Why: Names the proof (2,400 reviews), connects to the theme (looking how you used to), and the quote is from someone in the target persona.

**Evidence:**
- Weak: "A University Study Found 47% More Core Activation"
- Strong: "A University Study Found 47% More Core Activation Than Floor Planks, In Less Than Half The Time"
- Why: Names the evidence, then layers on the time advantage that serves the theme.

**Risk Reversal:**
- Weak: "Try It For 90 Days. Full Refund If Not Satisfied."
- Strong: "Try It For 90 Days. If You Don't See A Difference, Full Refund. No Questions."
- Why: "See a difference" connects to the theme (visible results). The guarantee removes friction for someone who's skeptical after failed attempts.

**Credibility:**
- Weak: "Built By A Physical Therapist"
- Strong: "Designed By A Physical Therapist Who Got Tired Of Telling Patients To Stop Doing Crunches"
- Why: The credential matters because it connects to what the audience has been doing wrong (crunches), which ties to the competitive framing.

## Sales Argument Types

Tag each point with its argument type during planning. Labels are for planning only and do not appear in the final copy.

### Mechanism

Lead: How the product works differently.
Body pattern: Describe the mechanism simply, explain why it matters for the theme, connect to the result the persona wants.

### Competitive

Lead: What this does that a specific alternative can't.
Body pattern: Name the competing approach that fails at THIS specific feature or benefit, state its limitation, show how this product solves it. The competitor changes per point depending on the feature being argued.

Examples of feature-specific competitors:
- Arguing "3 minutes" → compare against the gym (an hour plus commute, at least twice a week)
- Arguing "painless exercise" → compare against crunches/sit-ups (worsen back pain) or floor planking (wrecks wrists, you'll quit)
- Arguing "better padding" → compare against other planking boards (inferior padding) or planking on the floor (uncomfortable, painful, unsustainable)

Do not use a generic competitor for all points. Comparison tables encouraged when side-by-side makes the argument land faster than prose.

### Social Proof

Lead: What real customers experienced.
Body pattern: Specific numbers, then a real quote or recurring theme from reviews. Prioritize reviews from people who match the target persona and theme.

### Evidence

Lead: What was tested, measured, or proven.
Body pattern: Name the source, state the finding in one line, explain why it matters for the theme. Only use if Gate 0 proof flag = Yes.

### Risk Reversal

Lead: What happens if it doesn't work for them.
Body pattern: State the guarantee clearly, remove the friction, make trying feel safe. Frame it in terms of the outcome the theme promises.

### Credibility

Lead: Why the brand/product can be trusted.
Body pattern: Name the credential, explain why it matters for this specific theme and audience, connect to quality or design.

## Point Structure by Zone

### Zone 1 (Calm Trust)

1. Lead with a fact or insight
2. Explain it simply
3. Connect to product as the practical path

### Zone 2 (Breakthrough/Capability)

1. Lead with what the reader gains
2. Explain briefly why/how it works
3. Connect to product as the enabler

### Zone 3 (Frustration/Recognition)

1. Lead with the symptom they recognize
2. Validate it in 1-2 sentences
3. Connect to product as the fix

### Zone 4 (Urgency)

1. Lead with the consequence
2. Make it visceral in 1-2 sentences
3. Connect to product as the intervention

## Point Structure by Mode

### Educational Mode

Teach first, product second. The insight is the star. The product appears after the reader has learned something.

### Direct Mode

Problem first, product-as-fix immediately. The validation is the star. The product closes each point.

## Point Copy Rules

1. One sentence per line. No stacking sentences into paragraphs.
2. Each point makes ONE distinct argument. If it's making two, split it.
3. Don't assume the reader knows jargon. If they wouldn't say it to their friend, explain it.
4. Real testimonial quotes encouraged for social proof arguments.
5. Comparison tables encouraged for competitive arguments (see page-structure.md for table rules).

## Body Copy and Adjacent Benefits

Point body copy can include adjacent benefits (relevant to the persona but not core to the theme) as natural add-ons within a point, but only when they genuinely complement the core argument being made. They should feel like a bonus, not a distraction.

Example: A point arguing "3 minutes vs. an hour at the gym" could mention in the body that "it's also easier on your joints" if the persona is men over 40. The headline stays on-theme. The body adds a relevant bonus.

Adjacent benefits should never take over a point or appear in point headlines.

## Theme Coherence

Every point must serve the theme expressed in the main headline. Before finalizing any point, test it: would this point feel at home in this listicle, or does it read like it wandered in from a different one?

Each point must also map to at least one ad angle driving traffic to the listicle. The reader who clicks an ad about a specific topic should find that topic addressed within the first 1-2 points they see.

If the listicle serves multiple ad angles, the points should cover the full range of themes so any entry point feels relevant.
