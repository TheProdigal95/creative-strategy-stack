# Headline and Subheadline Formulas

## The Headline Expresses the Theme

The main headline is the theme made concrete and scroll-stopping. By this point, the theme (Gate 2) and the zone (Gate 3) are already decided. The headline's job is to express that theme through the emotional register of the zone.

Everything in the listicle, every point headline, every argument, every benefit, flows from what the main headline promises. If a point doesn't serve the headline, it doesn't belong.

## Headline Requirements

Every headline must include:

1. **Problem callout** — name the problem the audience actually experiences, using language from Gate 1 customer voice research. Not the clinical version, the version they'd say to a friend.
2. **Listicle format** — include the number of points (6-10).
3. **Competitive framing** — reference what they're switching from, what isn't working, or what they've already tried. This can be implicit ("why [common approach] fails") or explicit ("vs. [competing product/method]").
4. **Product reference** — optional but encouraged. Can be indirect: "this [product category]" or "one [product type]."

The headline must pass the scroll-stop test: would someone stop scrolling and tap this?
It must NOT read like ad copy. If it sounds like something a brand would say about itself, rewrite it as something an editor would publish.
No hard word limit, but every word must earn its place.

## Headline Formulas by Zone

These formulas are starting points, not rigid templates. Adapt them to express the specific theme.

### Zone 1 (Calm Trust)

- [Number] Things [Audience] Should Know About [Topic] (Before Trying [Common Approach])
- [Number] [Simple Truths/Facts] About [Problem] Most [Audience] Miss
- What [Timeframe] Of [Activity] Actually Looks Like (And Why [Common Approach] Falls Short)

### Zone 2 (Breakthrough/Capability)

- [Number] Things That [Timeframe] Of [Activity] Actually Does To Your Body
- [Number] [Results/Wins/Changes] You Get From [Simple Action] (That [Common Approach] Can't Match)
- What Happens When [Activity] Finally [Clicks/Works] After [Problem]

### Zone 3 (Frustration/Recognition)

- [Number] Signs [Problem Statement] (And Why [Common Approach] Isn't Fixing It)
- [Number] Signs [Your Activity] Is [Negative Outcome] (And What Actually Fixes It)
- [Number] Reasons [Common Approach] Isn't Working For [Problem]

### Zone 4 (Urgency)

- [Number] Warning Signs [Problem] Is Getting Worse (Despite [Common Approach])
- [Number] Signs [Problem] That Most People Ignore Until [Consequence]

## Subheadline Patterns by Zone

One to two lines. The subheadline bridges the headline to the first point and gives the reader a reason to keep scrolling.

### Zone 1 (Calm Trust)

Frames calm authority.
Example: "What the research says. And what actually helps."

### Zone 2 (Breakthrough)

Sets up the curiosity.
Example: "Here's what's actually happening when you [activity]."

### Zone 3 (Quiet Frustration)

Invites self-identification.
Example: "If even one of these sounds familiar, [problem statement]."

### Zone 4 (Urgency)

Raises stakes.
Example: "Ignoring these could cost you [consequence]."

The subheadline does not need to follow a rigid formula. It needs to bridge the headline to the first point and give the reader a reason to keep scrolling.
